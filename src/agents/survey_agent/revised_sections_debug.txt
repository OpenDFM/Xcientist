
Modern computer vision models exhibit a fundamental brittleness: despite high accuracy, they are highly susceptible to intentionally crafted, often imperceptible perturbations. This adversarial vulnerability undermines trust in safety-critical deployments like autonomous driving and medical diagnostics. The phenomenon stems from theoretical shortcomings in high-dimensional decision boundaries, explained by hypotheses like linear behavior or flattened decision regions. Furthermore, threats have evolved from digital artifacts to physical-world attacks, necessitating robust defenses and a deeper understanding of model geometry. The following subsections provide a detailed analysis of these foundational concepts, including historical context, machine learning foundations, threat taxonomies, and theoretical underpinnings.

#### Historical Context and Problem Formulation

The emergence of deep learning has catalyzed a paradigm shift in computer vision, enabling unprecedented performance across tasks ranging from image classification to complex scene parsing. However, this rapid advancement has exposed a fundamental and unsettling reality: these high-performing models are intrinsically brittle. While achieving, and often exceeding, human-level accuracy on curated datasets, deep neural networks (DNNs) exhibit a perplexing susceptibility to deliberate, imperceptible perturbations. This phenomenon, first rigorously documented in the context of image classification, marked the genesis of adversarial research. Early investigations synthesized in <Threat of Adversarial Attacks on Deep Learning in Computer Vision: A Survey> revealed a fundamental disconnect: a model’s conviction in its predictions is often orthogonal to human perception. An imperceptible perturbation, invisible to the human eye, can flip a model's classification with near certainty, a phenomenon first detailed in <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings>. This disconnect between high fidelity on clean data and vulnerability to noise established the adversarial arms race—a cyclical dynamic where the discovery of a vulnerability prompts a defense, which in turn inspires a more sophisticated attack.

The initial formulation of this problem centered on digital imperceptibility, often attributed to the high-dimensional linearity of DNN decision spaces. A critical insight from this body of work is the theoretical existence of these vulnerabilities. A notable empirical correlation cited across multiple reviews is that higher clean accuracy often associates with marginally better robustness, though rarely absolute immunity. However, this observation requires careful qualification. While high-capacity models (which often achieve high clean accuracy) are necessary for effective adversarial training, raw clean accuracy is frequently negatively correlated with empirical robustness on its own. This reveals a fundamental trade-off between standard generalization and adversarial robustness, suggesting that adversarial susceptibility is a distinct, higher-order property of model optimization rather than a mere symptom of poor generalization.

The transition from digital curiosity to physical threat fundamentally altered the gravity of the problem. It was no longer sufficient to view adversarial examples as artifacts of the loss landscape; the community had to confront their manifestation in the physical world. Early extrapolations of digital attacks into physical space—such as applying printed textures to stop signs—often failed due to environmental variables: lighting, camera angle, and sensor noise would destroy the precise pixel-level perturbations required by the algorithm. This challenge necessitated a revision of the threat model. Research documented in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey> outlines how the field moved toward "robust" physical attacks, often utilizing Expectation Over Transformation (EOT) techniques to optimize perturbations for worst-case environmental conditions. This gave rise to distinct categories of physical weaponry, including adversarial patches—localized, conspicuous artifacts that do not aim for stealth but rather for efficacy within a specific region—and optical attacks, which manipulate light before it reaches the sensor.

Furthermore, the physical domain introduced the critical concept of the "adversarial medium," defined as the physical carrier of the perturbation (e.g., clothing fabric, a printed poster, a projected light pattern). As explored in <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>, this carrier becomes a variable in the optimization process, expanding the scope of vulnerability from pure software to cyber-physical systems. This vulnerability extends beyond standard RGB imaging to other sensor modalities, such as infrared, where adversarial perturbations remain effective, challenging the assumption that different input representations naturally obfuscate the attack vector.

Parallel to the evolution of attacks, the understanding of *why* these adversarial phenomena occur has deepened, moving from simple linearity hypotheses to more complex geometric and manifolds-based theories. The field has largely converged on the observation that DNNs, despite their depth, effectively operate in a flattened, low-curvature region relative to the data manifold. Synthesized analyses, such as <Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review>, consolidate theories into linearity, data manifold, and regularization-based explanations, highlighting a crucial lack of consensus. This suggests that adversarial examples are an emergent property of the intersection of high-dimensional spaces and non-convex optimization landscapes rather than a single, fixable flaw. The consensus is that flat decision boundaries facilitate adversarial paths, a view supported by the effectiveness of decision-boundary-based attacks like DeepFool. Quantifying this geometric vulnerability, <Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks> proposes that the difficulty of finding adversarial examples correlates with the "steepness" of the loss landscape, introducing metrics like ACTS (Adversarial Converging Time Score) to measure this resistance.

These vulnerabilities have prompted the development of defense strategies, which have evolved dynamically alongside increasingly sophisticated attacks. The trajectory of defenses is a story of attrition, often described as a "whack-a-mole" scenario where early intuitive defenses were systematically dismantled. In contrast, Adversarial Training (AT), particularly robust optimization methods like those proposed in <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>, has emerged as the standard empirical baseline, though it comes at the cost of standard accuracy and significant computational overhead.

Beyond brute-force training, the community has explored detection and purification mechanisms. <Adversarial Perturbation Defense on Deep Neural Networks> surveys a wide array of these approaches, noting that detection mechanisms (e.g., distinguishing anomalous inputs based on feature squeezing or statistical divergence) are often brittle. A promising direction highlighted in this literature is the use of probabilistic models for defense. <Detecting Adversaries, yet Faltering to Conditional Variational AutoEncoders> demonstrates that Conditional Variational Autoencoders (CVAEs) can effectively detect adversaries by capturing the underlying distribution of benign data. This approach leverages the insight that adversarial perturbations, despite having small Lp-norms, distort the latent representation significantly, causing a distributional mismatch that CVAEs can flag. However, the "arms race" persists; for every detection mechanism, there exists a circumvention strategy designed to mimic the benign distribution.

The evolution of adversarial robustness is now intersecting with advancements in model architecture. The dominance of CNNs is being challenged by Vision Transformers (ViTs) and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), both of which introduce new robustness vectors. <A Survey and Evaluation of Adversarial Attacks in Object Detection> notes that while ViTs might offer global context, their patch-based processing makes them uniquely vulnerable to adversarial patches that align with token boundaries. Conversely, GNNs treat images as relational graphs, modeling non-local dependencies and topological structures, as detailed in <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision>. This paradigm shift allows for robustness by anchoring predictions in global structural consistency rather than local pixel correlations.

Finally, the pursuit of robustness has led to the bifurcation of defense research into "empirical" and "certified" defenses. Empirical defenses, like adversarial training, are effective against known attacks but offer no mathematical guarantee against future ones. Certified defenses, conversely, aim to provide provable guarantees of robustness within a specific threat model (e.g., a specific $\epsilon$ ball around an input). <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey> highlights the rise of randomized smoothing as a dominant technique for certification. By adding noise to the input and taking a majority vote over multiple samples, one can derive a certified radius within which the prediction is guaranteed to remain consistent. While these methods provide rigorous bounds, they often come with high computational costs and potentially significant drops in clean accuracy, representing the current frontier of the trade-off between utility and security.

In conclusion, the historical trajectory of adversarial examples reveals a shift from theoretical limitation to practical crisis. However, viewing this phenomenon merely as a security flaw requiring patching is insufficient. The evidence suggests that adversarial vulnerability is a fundamental property of high-dimensional linearity coupled with gradient-based optimization. As works like <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings> and <Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review> highlight, the very mechanisms that enable deep learning's success—differentiability and optimization in high-dimensional spaces—are the same mechanisms that facilitate efficient adversarial manipulation. Therefore, adversarial examples are not simply a bug to be fixed, but an inherent characteristic of the learning paradigm itself, demanding a fundamental rethinking of the objective function rather than just defensive add-ons.

#### Machine Learning Foundations in Computer Vision

The evolution of computer vision is defined by a shift from rigid, manually engineered rules to flexible, data-driven representation learning. Unlike classical methods, Deep Learning models, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), utilize an optimization process—backpropagation with Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)—to automatically learn optimal feature representations across layers. As highlighted in <Machine Learning in Computer Vision: A Review>, deep learning models are essentially "function approximators" driven by these gradient-based updates.

This transition from "hand-crafted" to "learned" features is the critical foundation for adversarial vulnerability. Because CNNs are designed to be differentiable for optimization, they inherently possess a continuous gradient connecting input perturbations to output changes. This mathematical property, which enables the automated discovery of complex features, simultaneously guarantees that a path exists to efficiently manipulate model predictions via small input modifications. Consequently, the brittleness of deep learning is not merely a symptom of poor generalization but a direct consequence of the gradient-based optimization that defines its learning process, as detailed in <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>. This vulnerability is further compounded by the high-dimensional nature of the feature space, a concept explored in <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings>, which demonstrates how the linear behavior of DNNs in these spaces facilitates the creation of adversarial samples.

#### Taxonomy of Adversarial Scenarios

The landscape of adversarial threats in computer vision is multifaceted, necessitating a structured taxonomy to understand the capabilities, constraints, and intentions of potential attackers. This section outlines the primary threat dimensions, including the attacker's knowledge (white-box vs. black-box), their objective (targeted vs. untargeted), and the modality of the attack (digital vs. physical).

#### Theoretical Underpinnings of Vulnerability

The vulnerability of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) to seemingly imperceptible perturbations stems from conflicting theoretical explanations, primarily the 'Linear Hypothesis' and the 'Manifold/Data Distribution' hypothesis. The Linear Hypothesis, detailed in <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings>, posits that DNNs behave linearly in high-dimensional spaces, allowing small perturbations to accumulate along directions that significantly alter the output. Conversely, the Manifold Hypothesis, synthesized in <Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review>, suggests that adversarial examples lie far from the natural data manifold but close to the decision boundary, effectively exploiting the model's flattened decision regions. The lack of consensus between these theories suggests that vulnerability arises from the intersection of high-dimensionality and non-convex optimization landscapes, rather than a single, fixable flaw. To rigorously quantify these geometric properties, recent work has proposed metrics like the Adversarial Converging Time Score (ACTS), as detailed in <A Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks>, which measures the 'steepness' of the loss landscape as a proxy for robustness.


This section synthesizes the evolution of adversarial attack methodologies, tracing the trajectory from theoretical white-box exploits to practical, constraint-driven strategies. The progression reveals a fundamental shift from establishing baselines with gradient-based attacks (FGSM, PGD) to prioritizing query-efficient methods (ZOO, Greedy Local Search) that obviate the need for substitute models. Furthermore, the field has transitioned from digital curiosity to scalable, physically realizable threats (e.g., EOT), a trajectory thoroughly mapped in <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>. This evolution highlights a convergence towards robustness challenges in real-world deployment, necessitating defenses that withstand the adversarial arms race.

#### Gradient-Based and Optimization Attacks

The landscape of gradient-based and optimization attacks constitutes the bedrock of adversarial research in computer vision, providing the mathematical machinery to stress-test deep neural networks. These attacks exploit the differentiable nature of deep learning models, primarily utilizing first-order optimization techniques (requiring gradient access) to craft perturbations that are imperceptible to the human eye but devastating to machine intelligence. By synthesizing insights from foundational surveys such as <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> and <Generation and Countermeasures of adversarial examples on vision: a survey>, alongside crucial empirical studies, we can trace the evolution from rudimentary gradient ascent to sophisticated iterative algorithms. This progression highlights a fundamental trade-off: the balance between the computational cost of generating an attack and its potency, or stealth, in the physical and digital domains.

The canonical entry point into gradient-based attacks is the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>, which generates adversarial examples using a linear approximation of the loss function. The perturbation is calculated as $\epsilon \cdot \text{sign}(\nabla_x J(\theta, x, y))$, aiming to maximize the loss within a bounded $L_{\infty}$ norm. However, relying on a single linear step is often insufficient for crafting imperceptible perturbations or overcoming robust models, a limitation discussed in <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>. This drove the evolution towards iterative refinement methods, epitomized by the Basic Iterative Method (BIM) and the Projected Gradient Descent (PGD). PGD, in particular, is described as the "strongest first-order attack" and has become the de facto standard for both attacking models and defending them via adversarial training. The consensus in the literature is that iterative methods yield significantly stronger, more imperceptible perturbations than single-step approaches because they effectively navigate the curvature and high-dimensional geometry of the loss surface. This geometric necessity is formally explored in studies like <A Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks>, which highlight that linear approximations (like FGSM) fail to capture the non-linear complexity of decision boundaries, necessitating the iterative traversal of PGD. This trajectory of refinement is well-documented in surveys such as <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey>.

While gradient descent methods are potent in white-box settings where model parameters are known, the field has seen a surge in optimization-based attacks designed for black-box scenarios. The seminal work <ZOO: Zeroth Order Optimization Based Black-box Attacks to Deep Neural Networks without Training Substitute Models> introduced a paradigm shift by treating the model as an oracle and estimating gradients via finite differences, effectively performing coordinate descent on pixel values. This approach avoids the "transferability gap" inherent in substitute model attacks but comes at a high query cost. Subsequent research has focused on reducing this burden, with methods like <Simple Black-Box Adversarial Perturbations for Deep Networks> demonstrating that even greedy local search algorithms—perturbing single pixels or small image regions based on gradient estimation—can reveal deep vulnerabilities. This suggests that the reliance on dense, global perturbations might not be strictly necessary for success; rather, localized disruptions to critical features can be just as effective.

The trade-off between perturbation density and attack success is further illuminated by sparse and localized attacks. The <Sliding Window Gradient Sign Attack> provides a compelling example where the gradient sign concept is applied within a sliding window framework to localize perturbations to specific facial features, such as the nose and eyes. This reveals an intriguing vulnerability: deep models may rely disproportionately on small, localized semantic regions for classification, making them susceptible to targeted spatial disruptions. This localized sensitivity suggests that adversarial effects need not be globally distributed to be effective. Furthermore, the pursuit of minimal perturbations leads directly to optimization attacks that formulate the adversarial process as a constrained minimization problem. The Carlini and Wagner (C&W) attack is a prominent example of this family, optimizing for the smallest $L_2$ or $L_0$ norm perturbation necessary to force misclassification. Unlike the "untargeted" noise of FGSM, <Generation and Countermeasures of adversarial examples on vision: a survey> highlights that C&W and its variants (like EAD) prioritize stealth and specificity, often achieving 100% success rates against undefended models with perturbations that are virtually invisible.

A major trend in optimization attacks is the move towards query efficiency and the handling of ensemble models. Attacks like <ZOO: Zeroth Order Optimization Based Black-box Attacks to Deep Neural Networks without Training Substitute Models> and other sparse perturbations underscore the fragility of DNNs, proving that even a single bit-flip can cause misclassification if placed strategically. This fragility is often exploited in black-box settings via transfer attacks, where an attacker trains a substitute model and transfers the adversarial examples. However, <Generation and Countermeasures of adversarial examples on vision: a survey> highlights that this transferability is rarely perfect and often relies on the unreasonable assumption that the attacker knows the target model's training data. To circumvent this, modern query-free attacks utilize zeroth-order optimization or decision-based boundaries, approximating the gradient without querying the model directly. This shift reflects a maturation of the field, moving from relying on the specificities of a single model to designing attacks with universal properties.

Generative models have also entered the optimization arena, moving beyond perturbing existing images to generating adversarial instances from scratch. As detailed in <Generation and Countermeasures of adversarial examples on vision: a survey>, methods have evolved from Adversarial Autoencoders (ATN) to GAN-based attacks, and recently to diffusion model-based attacks (AdvDifuser). These methods shift the optimization target from the pixel space to the latent space, allowing for the generation of adversarial noise that follows the semantic distribution of the clean image. This evolution highlights a critical trade-off between **transferability** and **realism**: while generative attacks often achieve superior stealth and speed, they may suffer from lower transferability to unseen models compared to optimization-based methods. Conversely, attacks starting from pure noise often generalize better but lack the realism of style-transfer based approaches. While this offers superior stealth and speed (once trained), it introduces new complexities: generative defenses often attempt to "purify" images back to the manifold, creating a new arms race where attacks are increasingly tailored to bypass the intermediate steps of diffusion-based purifiers.

Furthermore, the success of optimization attacks is deeply tied to the geometry of the decision boundary. <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings> formally introduced the concept that high-dimensional linear behavior is a root cause of vulnerability, suggesting that decision boundaries are often too linear to capture the complexity of the data distribution. Optimization attacks like  and PGD exploit this by finding the shortest path to cross these boundaries. However, the community is increasingly aware that standard $L_p$ metric optimization (minimizing $L_2$ or $L_\infty$ distance) may not correlate well with human perception. The survey <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> critiques the reliance on these metrics, noting that a perturbation with a low $L_2$ norm can still be visually obvious if it alters high-frequency components, whereas high-norm low-frequency changes might be ignored. Consequently, optimization attacks are slowly evolving to incorporate feature-space losses or perceptual metrics, attempting to align the mathematical minimization of perturbation with the semantic reality of the image.

The proliferation of these optimization strategies has forced a re-evaluation of what constitutes a "hard" attack. As noted in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey>, "adaptive attacks"—attacks specifically designed to break a particular defense—are now the gold standard for evaluation. For instance, when evaluating defenses based on input transformation (like JPEG compression), optimization attacks must include these differentiable approximations in their gradient calculation to succeed. This reality check has tempered the optimism around many proposed defenses. The comparative study in <A Comparative Study on Adversarial Attacks and Defense Mechanisms> empirically demonstrates how iterative gradient-based attacks consistently outperform single-step ones across various datasets, reinforcing the principle that attack potency is a function of iterative refinement.

#### Black-Box Attacks: Beyond Substitute Models

This subsection explores the paradigm shift in black-box adversarial attacks, moving beyond the reliance on training substitute models to more direct, query-based optimization strategies. Early black-box threat models largely depended on the concept of transferability, where an adversary trains a local "substitute" model and hopes that adversarial examples crafted on this proxy generalize to the target model. As identified in comprehensive surveys like <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey>, this approach suffers from a fundamental "transferability gap," resulting in significantly lower success rates compared to white-box attacks. The susceptibility of deep networks to gross perturbations, such as the single-pixel manipulations detailed in <Simple Black-Box Adversarial Perturbations for Deep Networks> and theoretical limits explored in <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings>, further underscored the brittleness of these models, even with minimal adversary knowledge. However, realizing the limitations of transfer-based methods, the research community pivoted toward gradient-free, query-based algorithms that treat the target model as a strictly opaque oracle. 

At the forefront of this shift is the <ZOO: Zeroth Order Optimization Based Black-box Attacks to Deep Neural Networks without Training Substitute Models> approach. ZOO eliminates the need for a surrogate model entirely by treating the target DNN as a continuous function and directly estimating gradients using finite differences. This method utilizes a zeroth-order stochastic coordinate descent algorithm, querying the target model twice per pixel (or batch of pixels) to estimate the gradient direction. The brilliance of ZOO lies not only in its theoretical elegance but also in its practical acceleration techniques, such as dimensionality reduction (optimizing a low-resolution version of the perturbation and upsampling) and importance sampling. By rigorously prioritizing high-variance pixels, ZOO significantly reduces the immense query burden usually associated with finite-difference methods. Consequently, ZOO achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art white-box attacks like the C&W attack, effectively democratizing white-box-level efficacy for adversaries with only black-box access.

While ZOO relies on coordinate-wise optimization, other gradient-free methods explore the search space more structurally. The Greedy Local Search algorithm presented in the same lineage of research, notably in <Simple Black-Box Adversarial Perturbations for Deep Networks>, offers a surprisingly effective and conceptually simple alternative. This algorithm focuses on optimizing a sparse set of pixels, iteratively probing a local neighborhood around previously perturbed coordinates. In each round, it identifies which pixel perturbation most aggressively reduces the probability of the true class and applies a small, "safe" perturbation to those coordinates. This approach validates that deep networks possess "critical pixels"—localized regions where a small perturbation causes catastrophic failure. This method extends the threat model to "k-misclassification," ensuring the true label does not appear in the top-k predictions, thereby breaking a more robust definition of classifier reliability. Both ZOO and Greedy Local Search share a common theoretical insight: deep neural networks, despite their depth, exhibit a decision boundary that is surprisingly accessible via derivative-free optimization.

However, the high query volume required to approximate these gradients remains a critical vulnerability and a primary cost metric. The survey <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> highlights a critical disparity: while query-based attacks offer superior effectiveness, they generate massive query traces (often 20K–100K for imperceptible perturbations on ImageNet), which can trigger rate-limiting defenses on commercial APIs. This contrasts with transfer-based attacks, which require no queries but suffer from a significant "transferability gap," resulting in lower success rates compared to white-box attacks. This dynamic defines the central tension of the modern black-box threat model: the trade-off between the high resource cost and detectability of query-based optimization (e.g., ZOO) versus the lower success rate of stealthier, query-free transfer attacks.

Consequently, the literature is pivoting towards hybrid approaches that aim to balance these extremes—leveraging transferability for initial footholds while employing limited, query-efficient refinement to bridge the success gap without incurring prohibitive costs.

Furthermore, the landscape of these gradient-free attacks is continuously evolving to handle more complex architectures and constraints. As deep learning applications expand into graph-structured data and relational reasoning, the vulnerabilities exposed by simple pixel attacks persist in new forms. For instance, the vulnerability of models in autonomous driving, specifically their reliance on relational structures, is explored in <Deep learning for object detection and scene perception in self-driving cars: Survey, challenges, and open issues>. While this survey focuses on object detection, the underlying vulnerability to adversarial patch attacks (a form of spatialized perturbation) echoes the findings of localized attacks in image classification. Moreover, the emergence of sophisticated Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and hybrid architectures like <MedicoCNN-GNN: A Hybrid Model with Integrated Attention Mechanism for Medical Image Classification> attempts to mitigate these risks by capturing inter-feature relationships. However, these advanced models are not immune; the theoretical underpinnings of vulnerability discussed in  suggest that any model relying on high-dimensional decision boundaries remains susceptible. The failure of early gradient-masking defenses proved that obfuscating gradients is insufficient; the direct estimation methods employed by ZOO and local search expose that the gradient information, while not directly visible, is never truly absent—it merely requires a different method of extraction.

In synthesizing these developments, the evolution from substitute models to query-based optimization marks a shift from exploiting model architecture to exploiting the fundamental geometry of the decision boundary. The <ZOO: Zeroth Order Optimization Based Black-box Attacks to Deep Neural Networks without Training Substitute Models> approach demonstrates that high-dimensional gradients can be estimated with finite differences, effectively treating the target model as a differentiable oracle despite zero knowledge of its internal parameters. This is complemented by the structural efficiency of the Greedy Local Search algorithm in <Simple Black-Box Adversarial Perturbations for Deep Networks>, which reveals that deep networks exhibit a 'critical pixel' phenomenon where localized, sparse perturbations are sufficient to induce misclassification. The implication, as underscored by <A Comprehensive Review of Adversarial Attacks and Defense Strategies in Deep Neural Networks>, is that robustness cannot rely on gradient obfuscation or architectural secrecy. Instead, defenses must assume the adversary can reconstruct the loss landscape through querying, necessitating a move towards intrinsic robustness rather than obscurity.

#### Sparse and Localized Perturbations

Sparse and localized perturbations challenge the assumption that adversarial examples require global noise, revealing that deep neural networks are hypersensitive to isolated, high-magnitude changes. These attacks demonstrate that manipulating only a minuscule fraction of input data—often targeting specific semantic regions or critical pixels—can effectively induce misclassification. This subsection explores the theoretical underpinnings and practical implementations of these threats, highlighting their implications for model robustness and the potential for physical-world exploits.

#### Generative Adversarial Attacks

The evolution of adversarial attacks has progressively shifted from the simple perturbation of existing images towards the comprehensive generation of entirely new instances using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). This shift represents a significant paradigm change in the threat landscape, moving beyond imperceptible noise injections to the creation of synthetic, yet highly realistic, adversarial examples that can deceive even robustly trained models. The transition from optimization-based attacks, such as the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) or Projected Gradient Descent (PGD), to generative approaches marks a crucial development in the study of Deep Learning vulnerabilities.

The fundamental limitation of perturbation-based attacks is their heavy reliance on access to the target model or the optimization process for every input instance. While effective in white-box settings, these methods struggle in scenarios requiring mass generation of attacks or high transferability. Generative Adversarial Attacks address this by training a dedicated generator network to produce adversarial examples in a single forward pass. This approach allows for the creation of a universal perturbation generator that, once trained, can apply effective perturbations to arbitrary inputs, thereby introducing a scalable threat vector. The synthesis of research indicates that generative models learn to transform clean images into adversarial counterparts in a highly efficient manner, often achieving high fidelity without iterative optimization per image.

The core of generative attacks lies in their ability to learn a mapping from a latent space to adversarial examples. This is often achieved through architectures like Adversarial Autoencoders (ATNs) or Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). These models can be trained to produce targeted noise that, when added to an image, causes misclassification, or to generate entirely new, realistic-looking instances that are inherently adversarial. The key distinction is between generators that produce pixel-level perturbations and those that synthesize realistic instances from scratch, both aiming to exploit the model's decision boundaries.

The literature highlights a distinct trade-off between **transferability** and **realism** in the context of generative attacks. Attacks starting from pure noise or random latent vectors often exhibit high transferability because the generator learns a universal mapping that generalizes well to unseen models. However, these attacks frequently suffer from a lack of realism, manifesting as abstract, noisy patterns. Conversely, attacks that start from real images and encode perturbations in a constrained latent space (often termed "StyLe-AT" or style-transfer based attacks) achieve superior realism, introducing perturbations that resemble natural variations like texture changes or lighting shifts. This approach has been shown to produce perturbations that are robust to geometric transformations and image compression.

Furthermore, the deployment of generative attacks raises new concerns regarding **black-box attacks**. Since a generative model produces adversarial examples in a feed-forward manner, it is inherently a black-box threat once trained. Transferability is less of an issue here because the generator is trained against a specific set of surrogate models but produces malicious inputs that affect others. The literature notes that generative attacks are particularly effective against commercial computer vision APIs (e.g., Google Cloud Vision, Amazon Rekognition) where query access is restricted or costly, as the generator can produce adversarial payloads at scale.

However, the rise of generative adversarial attacks has necessitated the development of more sophisticated defenses. While adversarial training remains a standard defense, it struggles against generative attacks that exploit manifold discontinuities. The "feature autoencoder" approach attempts to reconstruct the perturbed latent space, effectively delegating the defense to a purification step. Moreover, the concept of "generative adversarial training" has been proposed where a generator and a discriminator (the target model) are trained in an adversarial game to produce robust decision boundaries, effectively hardening the model against generative perturbations.

The implications of generative adversarial attacks extend into the theoretical understanding of deep learning robustness. These attacks challenge the assumption that decision boundaries are smooth and convex. By generating entire images, researchers can explore the high-dimensional manifold of the input space, identifying pockets of vulnerability that are inaccessible to perturbation-based methods. This has led to new theoretical insights into the "linearity hypothesis" of neural networks, suggesting that while local robustness can be achieved via adversarial training, global robustness remains elusive because generative models can traverse the manifold to find alternative paths to misclassification.

Unlike purely digital perturbations, which manipulate pixel values directly, physical attacks must navigate a complex transduction pipeline that includes material rendering, lighting variability, viewing geometry, and camera sensor characteristics, particularly the Image Signal Processor (ISP). This section explores the fundamental challenge of ensuring that adversarial perturbations generated in a digital optimization loop remain effective when manifested as tangible objects—such as stickers, projected light, or 3D-printed forms. The trajectory of research here has shifted from brute-force optimization to sophisticated strategies that account for physical realism, highlighting a distinct trend toward evaluating "adversarial mediums" as active constraints rather than passive carriers.

Furthermore, the evaluation of physical attacks demands a more holistic perspective than standard digital success rates, driving the adoption of multi-dimensional metrics that weigh stealth, practicability, and economic feasibility. #### Taxonomy of Physical Artifacts and Mediums

The transition of adversarial attacks from the digital domain to the physical world marks a pivotal shift in the threat landscape of computer vision, fundamentally altering the requirements for robust machine learning systems. While digital attacks manipulate pixel values directly in an image file, physical attacks must contend with the laws of optics, material properties, and the stochastic nature of the real-world environment. This necessitates the introduction of an intermediary entity—the **adversarial medium**—which serves as the tangible carrier of the perturbation. The nature of this medium is the primary determinant of an attack's stealth, efficacy, and generalizability. As we move beyond simple digital noise to 3D-printed objects and projected light, the distinction between "adversarial input" and "adversarial environment" blurs, creating a complex taxonomy of physical threats that can be broadly categorized into three distinct forms: adversarial patches (or stickers), optical attacks, and 3D-printed objects.

The most ubiquitous category within physical adversarial attacks is the **adversarial patch**, a localized, printable perturbation that is physically attached to an object to manipulate its classification. These patches can be further subdivided based on their semantic content and intended camouflage: meaningless patches and meaningful patches. Meaningless patches, such as those explored in seminal works like **** and ****, are essentially high-frequency noise patterns optimized to be effective over a range of transformations. While conceptually simple, they suffer from poor stealth and are easily flagged by human observers or anomaly detection systems. The trade-off here is stark: while they are easy to generate and highly effective in controlled settings, they lack the naturalism required for subtle attacks. This is observed in the analysis of **<Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>**, which notes that meaningless patches are printable and applicable to stationary objects but remain fragile across varying angles and distances.

In response, the field has pivoted toward **meaningful patches**, integrating adversarial perturbations into naturalistic objects like stickers, makeup, or stylized textures. These are designed to blend seamlessly into the scene, leveraging the generative capabilities of models like GANs to produce what **<Adversarial Cam>** and **** exemplify as "naturalistic camouflage." The introduction of these methods highlights the critical tension between stealth and efficacy; while meaningful patches significantly lower the "hiPAA" stealth score penalty compared to random noise, optimizing them becomes a harder, multi-objective problem. The initial survery of **<Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>** characterizes the risk profile of these patches as high due to their printability, yet the "Achilles' heel" remains their robustness to environmental factors. The **<Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>** further utilizes the "hiPAA" metric to quantify this, noting that while meaningful patches score higher on aesthetics and stealth (Ste., Aes.), they often struggle to match the raw effectiveness (Eff.) and robustness (Rob.) of more overt, but geometrically stable, attacks. This reveals that the physical medium itself imposes constraints on the solution space that pure digital optimization fails to account for.

Expanding beyond adhereable artifacts, **optical attacks** represent a class of threats that exploit the imaging pipeline rather than the object itself. These attacks often utilize **adversarial lights**, such as projected light or laser pointers, as seen in methods like **** and ****. A distinct advantage of optical attacks is their dynamic nature; unlike a static sticker, a projected pattern can be updated in real-time to compensate for viewing angle or object deformation. However, this dynamism comes at the cost of high sensitivity to ambient light and geometry, making them brittle in uncontrolled environments. Complementing this are attacks that **disturb camera imaging** directly, often through physical filters placed over the lens or by manipulating the Image Signal Processor (ISP). As noted in **<Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>**, even translucent stickers on a Tesla camera can induce severe artifacts. This introduces a hardware-level vulnerability that bypasses the neural network entirely, focusing on the pre-processing stage. The **<Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>** critiques these methods under the hiPAA framework, noting that while they achieve high stealth (often invisible to the naked eye), their practicability (Pra.) suffers due to the requirement for specific hardware calibration and proximity to the sensor.

The third major category, **adversarial 3D-printed objects**, introduces perturbations into the physical geometry of an object, primarily targeting depth perception or LiDAR sensors. Methods like **** demonstrate that by optimizing 3D meshes or textures, one can fool both monocular depth estimation and active sensing systems. Unlike 2D patches, these objects are inherently robust against resampling and geometric transformations that planar patches suffer from (e.g., occlusion, rotation). However, the barrier to entry is significantly higher; the "Economics" (Eco.) and "Practicability" (Pra.) dimensions of the hiPAA metric are often lower due to the cost and complexity of 3D printing. Furthermore, while they handle geometric robustness well, they are susceptible to material limitations—specifically, how light interacts with printed filaments. The survey by **<Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>** identifies the "simulator problem" here as particularly acute; the gap between a digital optimization in a physics engine (like CARLA) and the actual scattering of light off a 3D-printed surface is vast. Thus, the "adversarial medium" is not just a passive carrier but an active constraint that dictates the optimization landscape.

To navigate these trade-offs, researchers have proposed comprehensive evaluation frameworks. The introduction of **hiPAA** in **<Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>** is a watershed moment, shifting focus from a single metric (e.g., attack success rate) to a multi-dimensional view that includes **Stealthiness (Ste.)**, **Aesthetics (Aes.)**, **Practicability (Pra.)**, and **Economics (Eco.)**, alongside Effectiveness (Eff.) and Robustness (Rob.). This framework allows for comparative analysis across different mediums. For instance, a projected light attack might score high on Stealth (invisible) but low on Economics (requires expensive hardware), while a sticker patch scores high on Economics but low on Stealth. This taxonomy dissolves the illusion of a "universal" physical attack. The analysis within **<Adversarial Patch Attacks and Defences in Vision-Based Tasks: A Survey>** similarly highlights that making a patch visually natural (e.g., via QR codes or GAN textures) generally reduces its attack potency, reinforcing the Pareto frontier in the attack design space.

Furthermore, the physical manifestation of attacks introduces the challenge of **manufacturing constraints**. The loop from "Digital Perturbation Generation" -> "Adversarial Medium Manufacturing" -> "Threat Image Capturing" -> "Attacking" (the four-step framework from **<Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>**) highlights the transduction losses. For example, printer color limitations are quantified by metrics like NPS (Noise Power Spectrum), and camera ISP effects are often modeled as part of the Expectation Over Transformations (EOT) framework. However, EOT is merely a statistical approximation and often fails to capture the "long tail" of real-world variability. The rise of "natural adversaries"—conditions like fog, rain, or shadows, as mentioned in **<Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>**—suggests that the future of physical attacks may not lie in adding artificial objects, but in finding "natural" patches that optimize the perturbation within the statistics of natural scenes, thereby bypassing human and algorithmic detectors.

The literature also serves as a critical reminder that physical attacks are not limited to visible spectra. The study **<Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems>** demonstrates that vulnerabilities persist in infrared data used for human detection. Here, the medium changes from visible ink to thermal properties, yet the fundamental reliance on gradient-based optimization often remains. This expands the taxonomy of physical mediums to include thermal camouflage materials, which the **<Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>** identifies as an emerging type of adversarial medium. The trade-offs in the infrared domain mirror those in the visible: there is a balance between the thermal "stealth" (making a heat signature disappear) and the physical visibility of the object to a human observer.

Finally, the move toward physical attacks has exposed the fragility of current defense mechanisms. **<Adversarial Patch Attacks and Defences in Vision-Based Tasks: A Survey>** categorizes defenses into empirical and certified approaches. Empirical defenses like detection and smoothing are often brittle against adaptive attacks that know the defense mechanism—this is the "adaptive attack" phenomenon referenced in **<Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey>**. Certified defenses, which provide mathematical guarantees within a perturbation radius (or patch size), represent the gold standard but often come with strict constraints on the shape and size of the attack (usually requiring square or convex shapes). They struggle to certify the irregular shapes of meaningful patches or the dynamic nature of optical projections. Therefore, the taxonomy of physical attacks reveals a cascading effect: as attacks evolve from simple patches to complex 3D-printed or optical vectors, the utility of standard off-the-shelf defenses diminishes, necessitating a fundamental re-evaluation of robustness certification in the continuous physical domain.

The evolution of physical adversarial mediums—from simple "" noise on a poster to "HL-HGAT" inspired relational reasoning for scene topology—demonstrates a trajectory toward attacks that are increasingly context-aware and geometrically robust. The **<Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems>** paper confirms that even non-visible modalities are susceptible to the same fundamental optimization principles, just on different substrates. Consequently, the future of securing computer vision systems lies not just in patching the model, but in hardening the physical-to-digital pipeline against this entire heterogeneous class of medium-based threats.

#### Optimization for Robustness (EOT)

Optimization for physical robustness in computer vision fundamentally grapples with the translation of adversarial perturbations from the pristine, noise-free environment of digital optimization to the chaotic, stochastic reality of the physical world. This transition is where most generative algorithms fail, necessitating sophisticated optimization paradigms like Expectation Over Transformations (EOT). Introduced to bridge the "simulator problem"—the significant domain gap between controlled validation environments such as CARLA and uncontrolled real-world performance—EOT represents a shift from seeking a single optimal perturbation to finding a distribution of perturbations that maximizes the probability of attack success under a variety of environmental transformations. However, despite these advancements, the literature reveals a persistent dissonance between simulation and physical reality, highlighting that even optimized attacks often rely on specific setups that do not generalize.

The core concept of EOT addresses the brittleness of standard gradient-based attacks when applied to physical objects. While foundational methods like <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings> established that DNNs are highly susceptible to imperceptible perturbations, subsequent research outlined in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey> demonstrates that these digitally constructed perturbations degrade rapidly when subjected to real-world noise. EOT attempts to resolve this by integrating a suite of stochastic transformations—such as geometric warping, lighting variations, and blur—directly into the optimization loop. The objective function is reformulated to maximize the *expected* attack success rate over these transformations, rather than the success on a static image. This approach is pivotal for generating adversarial patches or textures that must remain effective across variations in viewing angle and distance. For instance, <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey> notes that EOT is frequently paired with spatial robustness techniques, such as Thin Plate Spline (TPS) deformations, to simulate the realistic stretching and folding of materials like clothing or stickers. The "adversarial medium"—the physical carrier of the perturbation—becomes a variable in the optimization process. Whether the medium is a meaningless patch, as explored in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>, or a meaningful, naturalistic camouflage as seen in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey>, the optimization must account for how the medium interacts with the camera capture pipeline.

Despite the theoretical elegance of EOT, the literature highlights a significant challenge known as the "simulator problem." Validating attacks in simulators like CARLA, which are referenced in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>, provides scalability but introduces a domain gap. Simulators often lack the nuanced fidelity of real-world camera sensors, specifically the non-linearities introduced by the Image Signal Processor (ISP). The ISP pipeline—comprising debayering, white balance, and compression—acts as an undocumented non-linear transformation that can fundamentally alter adversarial perturbations. Consequently, an attack optimized via EOT within a perfect digital simulation may fail when printed and photographed, as the sensor’s response function differs from the simulation's assumptions. This gap is the central bottleneck in physical adversarial research. While <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey> attempts to systematize the evaluation of physical attacks through metrics like hiPAA (Effectiveness, Stealthiness, Robustness, Practicability, Aesthetics, Economics), acknowledging the infinite variables of the physical environment remains the "Achilles' heel" of EOT-based approaches. The cost of physical printing and testing further exacerbates this, driving the need for more robust optimization techniques that can theoretically account for these sensor-specific distortions.

The optimization landscape is further complicated by the differing objectives in the digital versus physical domains. In the digital realm, attacks often rely on high-frequency noise which is imperceptible to the human eye but highly effective against DNNs. However, in the physical world, such perturbations are conspicuous and aesthetically poor, violating the stealthiness requirement of hiPAA. Therefore, physical optimization shifts toward generating "meaningful" patches—real-world objects like stickers or shadows—that blend naturally with the scene. <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey> categorizes this as a trade-off between stealth and efficacy. Optimization algorithms must now navigate a dual objective: maximizing the loss of the target model while minimizing the visual artifact distance from a natural distribution. This often involves Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to synthesize realistic textures that survive the camera pipeline. For example, <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey> highlights that physical attacks are often conspicuous, necessitating style transfer techniques to embed perturbations into natural scenes. The optimization process becomes a balancing act—a failure to generalize results in an attack that is only visible to the model under specific, narrow conditions (e.g., a specific distance or lighting angle), rendering the EOT optimization brittle.

Furthermore, the choice of optimization objective function in EOT can be manipulated to yield different failure modes in physical systems. While standard EOT minimizes cross-entropy loss for misclassification, recent works explore optimizing for feature-space disruption. This is evident in the defense literature, where models like <Feature autoencoder for detecting adversarial examples> utilize autoencoders to reconstruct perturbed inputs. An advanced physical attack optimized via EOT would theoretically need to fool both the primary classifier and any intermediate feature detectors or pre-processing defenses (such as JPEG compression or smoothing) mentioned in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>. This leads to "adaptive attacks" where the optimization process explicitly includes the defense mechanism in the loop. However, <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey> warns that this often leads to "obfuscated gradients," where the optimization converges on paths that exploit weaknesses specific to the defensive pre-processing rather than the core model, a failure mode that translates poorly to real-world deployment where the exact defense parameters might be unknown or dynamic.

The quest for robustness via EOT has also intersected with the geometry of the loss landscape. <A Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks> proposes metrics (ACTS) that quantify robustness based on the convergence time to an adversarial example. This geometric perspective suggests that robust optimization is not just about adding noise but about understanding the local curvature of the model's decision boundary. In physical scenarios, the input manifold is effectively mapped through a chaotic function (the physical world) before reaching the model. EOT attempts to smooth this manifold, but the high variance of real-world measurements often requires optimizing over a massive set of transformations, which is computationally prohibitive. Consequently, many researchers resort to "sim-to-real" approximations, training on heavily augmented data that mimics the physical pipeline. However, as noted in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>, relying solely on simulators for validation is a major pitfall; the optimization might overfit to the artifacts of the simulator (e.g., perfect lighting physics) rather than learning transferable physical robustness.

Finally, the optimization strategies for physical robustness must contend with the evasion-based nature of attacks. As the threat model evolves from universal digital perturbations to localized, physical artifacts, the optimization constraints tighten. <Sliding Window Gradient Sign Attack> demonstrates that identifying localized vulnerabilities in specific facial features (eyes, nose) can be more efficient than global optimization, suggesting that spatially aware EOT variants might yield better physical results. Furthermore, the interaction between the optimization process and the hardware pipeline is bidirectional. Beyond simply coping with the camera pipeline, some research summarized in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey> explores disturbing the camera imaging directly (e.g., via optical filters). Here, the optimization targets the physics of light capture rather than the neural network weights, representing a reversal of the EOT paradigm where the "transforms" are the actual physical manipulations. As the field moves toward more complex architectures like Vision Transformers, noted in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey> as a shift in defense trends, the optimization for physical robustness will likely need to account for attention-based mechanisms, which may process tokenized patches differently than CNNs, requiring a new generation of EOT tailored to the quirks of transformer-based vision models.

#### Physical Attack Evaluation Metrics (hiPAA)

The evaluation of physical adversarial attacks has historically suffered from a narrow focus on a single metric: effectiveness, typically measured as a drop in model accuracy. While fundamental, this unidimensional assessment fails to capture the multifaceted reality of deploying an attack in the physical world. An attack that achieves a 99% failure rate in the target model but costs thousands of dollars to manufacture, is instantly visible to a human observer, or fails under varying lighting conditions is ultimately useless from a security engineering perspective. To address this deficiency, the community has moved toward holistic evaluation frameworks. The most prominent of these is the "hexagonal indicator of Physical Adversarial Attack" (hiPAA), introduced in <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>. This framework proposes six critical dimensions: Effectiveness, Stealthiness, Robustness, Practicability, Aesthetics, and Economics. By synthesizing literature across these dimensions, hiPAA provides a structured language to dissect the pervasive trade-offs that define the security landscape of physical deep learning systems.

**Effectiveness and Robustness: The Stability Paradox**

At the core of any adversarial evaluation lies **Effectiveness** (Eff.), quantifying the performance degradation induced on the victim model. In physical settings, this is often measured by the misclassification rate or the reduction in mean Average Precision (mAP) for detection tasks. However, raw effectiveness in a controlled environment is a misleading prometheus. The true challenge lies in **Robustness** (Rob.), or the stability of the attack against environmental variations. A physical patch that enters the camera's field of view is subject to geometric transformations—rotation, scaling, and translation—along with photometric changes like lighting and shadows. The survey in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey> highlights a significant "environmental robustness gap," noting that while digital attacks are highly optimized, their physical manifestations often falter due to unmodeled real-world variables.

To bridge this gap, researchers have employed strategies like the Expectation Over Transformations (EOT) framework, as discussed in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>, which incorporates random transformations during the optimization process to maximize expected robustness. However, even with EOT, the "simulator problem" remains a critical bottleneck. Validation in simulators like CARLA cannot fully capture the spectral characteristics of real camera sensors or the specific properties of printing materials. For instance, the <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey> notes that robustness is often achieved via brute-force augmentation rather than a learned simulation of the physical transduction pipeline. This results in trilemmas where patches may be effective and robust across angles but fail to be transferable across different surrogate models, or conversely, highly transferable digital perturbations that are brittle to simple geometric transformations in the physical world.

**Stealthiness and Aesthetics: The Human Element**

Beyond technical performance, physical attacks must contend with the human observer, necessitating metrics for **Stealthiness** (Ste.) and **Aesthetics** (Aes.). Stealthiness measures the invisibility of the artifact to a human eye, while Aesthetics measures its social acceptability and visual appeal. The literature documents a sharp dichotomy here. Early attacks like LaVAN utilized "meaningless patches"—random high-contrast patterns—which are highly effective but functionally impossible to conceal. In contrast, techniques such as  or  utilize generative adversarial networks (GANs) to synthesize "meaningful patches" that integrate naturally into the scene, such as makeup or realistic stickers.

However, achieving high stealthiness often comes at the cost of effectiveness. <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey> observes that "highly effective and cheap" methods (like standard AdvPatch) often score poorly on Stealthiness and Aesthetics because they rely on conspicuous perturbations. Conversely, niche methods attempting to be invisible, such as optical attacks or subtle texture modifications, often lack broad Practicability (Pra.). For example, attacks utilizing human-invisible infrared perturbations or polarization filters can be highly stealthy, yet their deployment is restricted to specific camera hardware, limiting their scope. Furthermore, related defenses like  or  explicitly exploit the lack of realism in many physical attacks by identifying localized regions that deviate from natural image statistics, thereby rendering the trade-off between stealth and detectability a central battleground in physical adversarial research.

**Economics and Practicability: The Deployment Bottleneck**

**Economics** (Eco.) and **Practicability** (Pra.) evaluate the feasibility of manufacturing and deploying an attack. These metrics are often overlooked in digital-centric surveys but are paramount in physical security. A sophisticated optimization might yield a geometric perturbation that requires nanometer-scale 3D printing or a specific high-lumen laser projector, rendering the attack practically unfeasible for a standard adversary. The inventory of "adversarial mediums" cataloged in <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>—ranging from cheap stickers and clothing to complex 3D-printed artifacts and optical lenses—directly influences these scores.

The literature suggests that high Practicability is often associated with attacks that minimize manufacturing complexity. For example, sticker-based attacks on road signs or clothing patches on pedestrians are high in Economics (low cost) but may suffer in Robustness due to the non-rigid nature of clothing. Conversely, attacks on the camera's Image Signal Processor (ISP), as explored in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>, might require specialized hardware access or pre-processing capabilities, scoring lower on general Practicability despite being invisible to the naked eye. The "hiPAA" score weighting reflects this reality: while Effectiveness is weighted highest (0.3), Robustness and Stealthiness are critical (0.2 each), and Economics/Practicability are non-negligible factors (0.1 each), acknowledging that a theoretical attack with zero practicability has zero real-world value.

**Synthesis and the Path Forward**

Applying the hiPAA framework reveals that almost no method in the literature scores highly across all six dimensions. <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey> demonstrates this empirically: patches are highly effective but stealthy and aesthetic nightmares; optical attacks are stealthy but lacking in robustness and practicability; and 3D-printed objects struggle with manufacturing complexity. Furthermore, the evaluation of physical attacks is complicated by the lack of standardized benchmarks. Unlike digital attacks evaluated on ImageNet, physical attacks suffer from a lack of reproducible environments, often relying on proprietary datasets or custom setups (e.g., , ).

Furthermore, as defenses mature toward certified guarantees—such as randomized smoothing or  for Vision Transformers—the attack landscape is pushing toward gray-box vectors and exploitation of non-robust features that survive physical transduction. The hiPAA metric also implicitly suggests future directions: a "hexagonal" physical attack must likely combine the cheapness of patches with the stealth of optical methods, optimizing for a multi-objective loss that directly minimizes human detectability while ensuring geometric stability. Without such holistic evaluation standards, as provided by hiPAA, the field risks progressing along fragmented axes, celebrating high accuracy in one dimension while ignoring catastrophic failure in physical deployment constraints.

#### Vulnerabilities in Infrared and Non-Standard Spectra

Adversarial machine learning has largely evolved as a field centered on the RGB domain, utilizing standard benchmarks such as ImageNet and CIFAR-10. However, the deployment of deep learning in safety-critical domains often necessitates sensor modalities that transcend the visible spectrum. Infrared (IR) imaging, particularly for applications like human presence detection and night surveillance, represents a critical non-standard domain. The provided survey outlines a clear progression: while the foundational threats are well-mapped in <Threat of Adversarial Attacks on Deep Learning in Computer Vision: A Survey> and <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey>, the specific vulnerabilities and defensive postures within these non-standard spectral domains remain less explored. This subsection investigates the unique threat landscape of infrared deep learning, synthesizing the limited but pivotal research that establishes the persistence of adversarial vulnerabilities across sensor modalities and the efficacy of transferable defense strategies.

Infrared imaging relies on thermal signatures rather than reflected visible light, operating on the principle that all objects above absolute zero emit radiation. One might hypothesize that this fundamental difference in data acquisition might offer inherent robustness against perturbations designed to fool RGB-based models. However, the nuclear paper in this domain, **Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems**, decisively challenges this assumption. The central insight of this research is that the convolutional operators driving modern deep learning extract hierarchical features that are vulnerable regardless of the spectral characteristics of the input. By reproducing the conditions of **Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks**, specifically the application of Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) against CNNs trained exclusively on infrared data, researchers have demonstrated that adversarial attacks significantly weaken classification and detection performance in the IR spectrum. This finding is profound; it suggests that the "robustness via domain shift" argument is invalid. The perturbations that degrade model confidence in visible images translate effectively to the thermal domain. This mirrors the "linear explanation" for adversarial examples discussed in **Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review**, suggesting that the high-dimensional linear behavior of decision boundaries is a property of the feature space learned by CNNs, not merely an artifact of RGB pixel statistics.

Furthermore, the threat space in non-standard spectra is not just about digital manipulation but physical realizability. The subsection **Taxonomy of Physical Artifacts and Mediums** within the broader survey highlights that physical attacks require a medium to carry the perturbation. In the context of infrared, this poses interesting constraints and opportunities for an attacker. While **Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey** details how patches and optical attacks operate in visible light, the interaction of materials with thermal radiation introduces different physics. The heat emission properties of an adversarial patch (the "medium") must be carefully engineered to deceive a thermal sensor. This is far more complex than printing a colored sticker. However, the foundational work in **Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems** proves that such vulnerabilities, at least in the digital-to-physical transfer sense, exist. They evaluated the response of object detection models on IR images, showing that bounding box prediction accuracy degrades under adversarial stress. This aligns with the broader findings in **A Survey and Evaluation of Adversarial Attacks in Object Detection**, which posits that detection architectures (which often share backbone feature extractors with classifiers) inherit the same susceptibilities. The "hybrid attack" proposed in the infrared security paper, which exploits common CNN bases for both classification and detection, underlines the systemic nature of these vulnerabilities. The model's inability to distinguish "human presence" from "adversarial noise" in the thermal band is a direct failure of semantic feature representation, a problem rooted in the architecture rather than the sensor.

The question of mitigation is equally critical. If infrared systems are vulnerable, are standard defenses effective? The literature suggests a strong affirmative regarding **Adversarial Training (<Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>)**. The infrared security study explicitly proves that adversarial training optimized for the infinity norm ($L_\infty$) increases the robustness of classification models trained on infrared data. This is a vital consolidation of the field. It implies that the "gradient mask" or "obfuscated gradients" issues discussed in **Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey** do not manifest differently in IR data; rather, standard adversarial training remains a viable baseline. However, the trade-offs associated with adversarial training—specifically the reduction in accuracy on clean data—must be evaluated against the operational requirements of the monitoring system. For a safety-critical system performing human presence detection, a slight degradation in benign performance might be an acceptable price for the substantial increase in security against an adversary attempting to mask a human presence.

The vulnerabilities in non-standard spectra must also be contextualized within the broader landscape of **Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey**. This survey categorizes attacks based on the "adversarial medium." In IR, the medium is constrained by thermodynamics. An adversary cannot simply print a pattern that radiates "cold" to hide a human; rather, they might use materials with specific emissivity to mimic the background or obscure the target. The vulnerability here is two-fold: adversarial noise added to the digital inference process, and physical obfuscation that reduces the signal-to-noise ratio of the thermal signature. The work in **Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems** serves as the bridge between these two worlds, establishing the digital vulnerability as a precursor to physical feasibility.

It is crucial to recognize that the "non-standard" nature of the data does not eliminate the fundamental paradox of deep learning identified in **The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings**. The existence of adversarial examples is often described as an "inherent weakness" of deep learning. The successful application of PGD in the infrared domain serves as strong evidence for this hypothesis. If the weakness were inherent to the statistical distribution of RGB images (e.g., relying on chromatic aberrations or specific texture patterns unique to visible light), we would expect thermal images to behave differently. The fact that they do not suggests the vulnerability lies in the continuous nature of the high-dimensional mapping learned by DNNs. This insight directs researchers away from "sensor-specific" fixes and toward "architecture-specific" robustness.

Looking forward, the integration of these findings suggests that securing monitoring systems in non-standard spectra requires a holistic approach. We cannot rely on the sensor modality as a defense barrier. Instead, systems must employ **Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World** strategies tailored to the specific constraints of IR. This might involve input preprocessing (though, as noted in **Input Pre-processing and Purification**, these are often fragile to adaptive attacks) or, more promisingly, detection of adversarial inputs. The concept of **Detecting Adversaries, yet Faltering to Noise? Leveraging Conditional Variational AutoEncoders** suggests that compact representations of inputs can distinguish benign anomalies (sensor noise) from adversarial perturbations. Applying such detection mechanisms to IR streams could provide a necessary secondary layer of verification, ensuring that the "human presence" detected by the model is actually a human, and not an adversarially crafted thermal signature.

In conclusion, the exploration of adversarial robustness in infrared and non-standard spectra confirms that the threat landscape of computer vision is pervasive. The work in **Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems** is foundational, proving that the vulnerabilities observed in standard RGB settings are not artifacts of visible light data but fundamental properties of the deep learning models applied to safety-critical tasks. The persistence of these vulnerabilities mandates that the defense-in-depth strategies outlined in **A Comprehensive Review of Adversarial Attacks and Defense Strategies in Deep Neural Networks**, particularly adversarial training, be rigorously applied to non-standard modalities. Furthermore, the physical realizability of these threats in the thermal domain, as foreshadowed by the taxonomies in **Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey**, underscores the need for robust evaluation metrics like **hiPAA** that assess not just digital efficacy but physical stealth and robustness. As we extend computer vision into the dark and the unseen, we must carry with us the sobering lessons of adversarial robustness.

#### Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Relational Reasoning

The paradigm of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) represents a fundamental shift in computer vision, moving beyond the rigid locality of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to explicitly model complex relational structures. While CNNs excel at capturing spatial hierarchies defined by grid-like convolutions, they often struggle to represent non-Euclidean data or capture high-order interactions between distant entities in a scene. GNNs address this by treating visual inputs—whether they be superpixels in an image, joints in a skeleton, or regions in a graph—as nodes in a graph, enabling the propagation of information through message passing along edges. This capability is critical for tasks requiring relational reasoning, such as understanding interactions between objects ("human-object interaction"), modeling the connectivity of road networks, or analyzing the complex topological structures within medical images. The evolution of these architectures, from basic Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) to more sophisticated Graph Attention Networks (GAT), has facilitated revolutionary performance gains by allowing models to learn which neighbors are most relevant dynamically. As highlighted in the comprehensive review <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision: A Task-Oriented Perspective>, GNNs have been successfully deployed across diverse vision modalities, including 2D images, video, and 3D data, for tasks ranging from object detection to scene graph generation.

However, the effectiveness of standard GNNs is often constrained by their reliance on simple, pairwise edge definitions, which may fail to capture the rich, multi-order topological constraints present in many real-world visual domains. Addressing this gap, recent research has introduced higher-order network topologies, such as Simplicial Complexes, which allow for the definition of signals on not just nodes and edges, but also triangles and higher-dimensional simplices. A prominent innovation in this space is the <HL-HGAT: Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network via Hodge-Laplacian Operator>, which explicitly models data as a simplicial complex. This approach utilizes the Hodge-Laplacian operator to define spectral filters on these higher-order structures, enabling the network to learn signal representations that respect the intrinsic topology of the data, such as the closure relations in a mesh or the cyclic dependencies in a biological network. By incorporating Simplicial Projection (SP) operators and Simplicial Attention Pooling (SAP), <HL-HGAT: Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network via Hodge-Laplacian Operator> achieves multi-simplicial interaction, effectively fusing information across different dimensionalities. Empirical results demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms standard GNNs on complex tasks, such as logistics optimization and molecular regression, by uncovering structural relationships that node-based models miss, such as identifying distinct clusters of molecular accessibility in the ZINC dataset.

The integration of GNNs into broader computer vision architectures, particularly in hybrid models, has further amplified their utility, especially in specialized domains like medical imaging. Standard CNNs are powerful feature extractors but lack the ability to reason about the relationships between these features explicitly. By combining CNNs with GNNs, researchers can leverage the strengths of both: the CNN extracts spatial features, while the GNN models the relational dependencies among these features. For instance, <MedicoCNN-GNN: A Hybrid Model with Integrated Attention Mechanism for Medical Image Classification> proposes a hybrid architecture where a CNN backbone extracts features from medical images, which are then processed by a GNN to capture inter-relationships. This integration is supported by attention mechanisms (CSBAN) that prioritize critical spatial and channel dimensions, resulting in superior classification accuracy (92%) and enhanced interpretability via visualization techniques like Grad-CAM. This hybrid approach underscores a key trend: relational reasoning is not just a standalone task but a complementary layer that boosts the performance and explainability of existing deep learning pipelines.

In the context of autonomous driving and robotics, relational reasoning via GNNs has transitioned from object detection to comprehensive scene understanding. The work <TopoNet: Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes> illustrates this by introducing an end-to-end framework capable of abstracting traffic knowledge beyond mere perception. Autonomous systems require more than just identifying obstacles; they must understand the "road genome"—the connectivity of lanes and the assignment of traffic elements. <TopoNet: Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes> employs a scene graph neural network to model these complex relationships, utilizing a Scene Knowledge Graph (SKG) to differentiate prior knowledge. Furthermore, the challenge of graph structure learning is addressed by methods like <Genetic-GSL: Graph Structure Learning With Automatic Search of Hyperparameters Based on Genetic Programming>, which optimizes both the graph structure and hyperparameters dynamically to improve resistance against adversarial attacks and structural noise. As robots become more integrated into daily life, the robustness of their vision systems becomes paramount. The vulnerability of these models to adversarial perturbations is a critical concern, as explored in <Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision? Adversarial Examples Against the iCub Humanoid>; however, GNNs offer a pathway to robustness by enabling anomaly detection and classification with reject options, ensuring safety in environments where imperceptible attacks could pose physical threats.

#### Graph Structure Learning and Robustness

A primary bottleneck in the deployment of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) lies in the inherent reliance on the raw, often noisy, and human-constructed graph structures found in real-world data. Unlike traditional grid-like data (images or text), graphs are irregular, and the quality of the structural topology directly dictates the performance of the learned representation. <Graph Neural Network (GNN) in Image and Video Understanding Using Deep Learning for Computer Vision Applications> observes that while GNNs have achieved revolutionary performance across various domains, the quality of the underlying graph—whether it represents pixel affinities in an image or relationships in a social network—is rarely guaranteed. The fundamental assumption is that the provided adjacency matrix accurately reflects the true relationships between entities. However, in practice, these graphs are frequently corrupted by spurious edges (background noise in superpixel graphs) or missing connections (occlusions in sensor networks). These imperfections lead to noisy message passing, where node features are corrupted by irrelevant neighbors, resulting in degraded performance on downstream tasks such as node classification and link prediction. Consequently, a significant portion of modern GNN research has shifted focus from designing new aggregation architectures to the problem of Graph Structure Learning (GSL), which seeks to refine or learn the graph structure alongside the node embeddings.

State-of-the-art GSL methods have evolved beyond simple feature similarity metrics (such as thresholding Euclidean distances) to incorporating label information and optimizing structural priors. A prominent example of this evolution is <Graph Structure Learning With Automatic Search of Hyperparameters Based on Genetic Programming>, which introduces a Genetic-GSL framework. This approach recognizes that the graph structure is not an isolated variable; it is intrinsically linked to the hyperparameters of the GNN model (e.g., dropout rates, learning rates, and the strength of self-loops). Traditional hyperparameter optimization is computationally expensive and decoupled from structure learning. Genetic-GSL treats the graph structure and these hyperparameters as a composite "parent" gene pool. Through cycles of crossover and mutation, it dynamically evolves "offspring" configurations that yield higher node classification accuracy. This method effectively navigates the discrete search space of graph topologies alongside the continuous space of hyperparameters. The empirical results indicate that this dynamic fitting process not only improves node classification performance by approximately 1.2% over baselines but, crucially, enhances the model's resistance to adversarial attacks. This implies that a learned, "clean" structure acts as a form of implicit regularization, smoothing the decision boundary and making the model less sensitive to the perturbations introduced by adversarial examples. This holistic approach exemplifies a trend where graph construction is no longer a static pre-processing step but an integrated, learnable component of the neural architecture.

The drive to capture richer relational information has led to a paradigm shift from simple node-centric graphs to higher-order geometric deep learning, as embodied by <Advancing Graph Neural Networks with HL-HGAT: A Hodge-Laplacian and Attention Mechanism Approach for Heterogeneous Graph-Structured Data>. Rather than viewing data solely as a set of connected nodes, this framework models data as a simplicial complex, encompassing nodes (0-simplices), edges (1-simplices), and triangles (2-simplices). This topological rigor addresses the limitations of standard GNNs, which often struggle to define convolutional operations on edges or account for higher-order cycles without ad-hoc transformations like constructing line graphs. By utilizing the Hodge-Laplacian operator—a differential operator defined for simplicial complexes—HL-HGAT provides a mathematically grounded method for processing signals on these heterogeneous structures.

The innovation of HL-HGAT lies in its three core components: HL-filters, Simplicial Projection (SP) operators, and Simplicial Attention Pooling (SAP). The HL-filters operate in the spectral domain of the k-th Hodge-Laplacian, but to ensure scalability, they employ a polynomial approximation (specifically Laguerre polynomials) rather than expensive eigen-decomposition. This allows the model to maintain spatial localization (the receptive field depends on the polynomial order) while handling complex topologies. Furthermore, the SP operators allow for the fusion of information across dimensions—for example, projecting an edge-level signal (representing interactions between nodes) back onto the nodes to update their features. This multi-simplicial interaction is vital for tasks where relationships are as important as the entities themselves. Finally, the SAP mechanism introduces a novel pooling strategy. Unlike standard graph pooling that might rely solely on node centrality (e.g., Graclus clustering) or simple attention scores, SAP combines self-attention within simplices with cross-attention across simplices. This ensures that when the simplicial complex is coarsened, topologically significant components are preserved.

Empirically, HL-HGAT demonstrates that capturing this higher-order topology yields superior performance across diverse domains, from logistics (TSP routing) to neuroscience. For instance, in the ZINC molecular dataset, HL-HGAT uncovered a novel correlation between molecular structure and the Synthetic Accessibility Score (SAS), a clustering pattern missed by standard GNNs. Similarly, in brain age prediction, the attention maps generated by the model focused on neuroanatomically plausible regions (hippocampus and amygdala), providing interpretability that is often absent in "black-box" deep learning models. This suggests that enforcing topological consistency through the Hodge-Laplacian constraints guides the neural network to learn features that align with physical and semantic realities. However, the paper also highlights a lingering challenge in GSL and structural learning: the downsampling strategy in SAP currently relies on Graclus clustering (a topology-based method) rather than being fully learned. While this ensures topological consistency, it risks pruning simplices that are structurally minor but functionally important.

The tension between purely data-driven structure optimization and mathematically constrained topology extends to the broader literature of GNNs in computer vision. As detailed in <A Survey of Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision: A Task-Oriented Perspective>, the application of GNNs to visual data often involves transforming images or point clouds into graph structures. In these scenarios, the "noise" in the graph might represent irrelevant background superpixels or occluded object parts. A purely heuristic approach to graph construction might fail to distinguish between a foreground object and the noise. Conversely, methods like HL-HGAT, which impose strict topological constraints, ensure that messages are passed only along valid geometric paths. However, the optimal solution often lies in a hybrid approach. For example, in the medical domain, <MedicoCNN-GNN: A Hybrid Model with Integrated Attention Mechanism for Medical Image Classification> combines CNNs (for spatial feature extraction) with GNNs (for relational reasoning). In such hybrid architectures, the graph structure is usually defined by the spatial proximity or feature similarity of extracted patches. The robustness of the classification depends heavily on ensuring that the graph connects only biologically relevant regions. If the graph connects a tumor patch to healthy tissue due to noise, the GNN might average out the discriminative features, leading to misclassification.

Furthermore, the impact of graph structure robustness is not limited to static images but extends to dynamic and federated environments. In <Optimizing Federated Graph Learning with Inherent Structural Knowledge and Dual-Densely Connected GNNs>, the authors address the "non-IID" nature of graph data across different clients in a federated setting. Here, the "noise" can be considered the domain shift between clients. The proposed FedDense framework explicitly encodes structural knowledge alongside node features to bridge these shifts. This reinforces the idea that the graph structure carries domain-defining information. If the structural aggregation is not robust to the disparate distributions of different clients (i.e., if the graph structure is treated as a fixed, moving average of noisy inputs), the federated model fails to converge.

Therefore, the advancement of robust GNNs relies on a dual-track approach: learning the correct graph structure while respecting topological constraints. Genetic-GSL represents the adaptive track, allowing the model to "forget" noisy connections (e.g., through edge pruning) and adapt hyperparameters dynamically. HL-HGAT represents the consistent track, ensuring that whatever structure is learned is mathematically sound within the laws of geometry. Future directions in this field likely involve merging these concepts—for instance, using genetic algorithms to optimize the weighting of simplices in a Hodge-Laplacian framework or employing the learned attention weights of SAP to guide the downsampling process rather than relying on static clustering. By addressing the brittleness of the input graph structure, researchers are moving from simply processing graphs to actively constructing the optimal relational inductive bias for learning.

#### Hybrid Architectures and Attention Mechanisms

The evolution of deep learning in computer vision has largely been driven by the pursuit of higher accuracy on various perceptual tasks. Initial successes of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) established the dominance of spatial feature extraction, as detailed in surveys like <A survey of the recent architectures of deep convolutional neural networks>. However, as the field matured, a critical limitation of pure CNN architectures became apparent: their inherent inductive bias towards local pixel correlations often fails to capture global context, long-range dependencies, and structural relationships between disparate image regions. Consequently, the research landscape has shifted toward more sophisticated architectures that integrate complementary learning paradigms to enhance feature extraction capabilities, interpretability, and robustness. This shift is particularly evident in the convergence of CNNs with Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and attention mechanisms, creating hybrid systems that leverage the strengths of each component while mitigating their individual weaknesses.

The most prominent trend in overcoming the limitations of isolated architectures is the integration of attention mechanisms directly into convolutional pipelines. While  demonstrated the power of self-attention in NLP, translating this to vision requires addressing the quadratic complexity of attention maps relative to input size. Solutions like the Convolutional Spatial and Channel Block Attention (CSBAN) mechanism, as proposed in <MedicoCNN-GNN: A Hybrid Model with Integrated Attention Mechanism for Medical Image Classification>, illustrate a sophisticated approach to this problem. By prioritizing critical spatial and channel dimensions, CSBAN allows the CNN backbone to dynamically focus on discriminative features rather than treating the entire receptive field with equal weight. This is a significant advancement over standard CNNs, which suffer from information bottlenecks where less relevant spatial details are washed out by subsequent pooling and convolution operations. The success of such attention modules in <MedicoCNN-GNN>—achieving 92% accuracy in medical image classification—underscores the benefit of "feature map exploitation" through channel and spatial boosting. This aligns with the broader taxonomy of CNN innovations identified in <A survey of the recent architectures of deep convolutional neural networks>, which categorizes attention as a distinct architectural innovation designed to enhance the representational capacity of deep networks without necessarily increasing depth or width. The fundamental hypothesis is that localized attention allows the model to Inherit the translation invariance and efficient computation of CNNs while gaining the dynamic weighting capabilities of transformers.

However, attention mechanisms primarily address intra-feature weighting (i.e., which parts of an image are important). They are less effective at explicitly modeling inter-feature relationships or the structural topology of the data. This is where the integration of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) provides a complementary strength. As outlined in <A survey of graph neural networks: concepts, architectures, techniques, challenges, datasets, applications, and future directions>, GNNs excel at message passing and aggregating information across a defined topology. In the context of vision, this topology is often constructed by treating image features (e.g., superpixels, keypoints, or patch embeddings) as nodes in a graph. The hybrid model in <MedicoCNN-GNN> explicitly demonstrates this synergy: the CNN extracts spatial features, while the GNN processes these features as tabular and relational data to capture inter-relationships. This dual-stream approach effectively transforms the dense grid structure of an image into a sparse graph, enabling the model to reason about non-local relationships—such as the connectivity between anatomical structures in medical scans or the relational layout of objects in a scene. This paradigm shift is further explored in <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision: A Task-Oriented Perspective>, which highlights that hybridizing CNNs with GNNs allows computer vision systems to move beyond mere object detection toward scene graph generation and relationship understanding.

To further enhance the structural reasoning capabilities of GNNs, recent research has explored the integration of higher-order topological concepts, moving beyond simple node-edge graphs. The work <: Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network via Hodge-Laplacian Operator> represents a significant theoretical leap in this direction. Instead of representing data as a flat graph,  models data as a simplicial complex, encompassing nodes (0-simplices), edges (1-simplices), and triangles (2-simplices). This allows the model to capture not just pairwise relationships but higher-order connectivity, which is crucial for complex scene understanding. By employing the Hodge-Laplacian operator and attention mechanisms,  can learn heterogeneous signal representations across different topological dimensions. This is particularly relevant for vision tasks where occlusion or ambiguity requires reasoning about the structural completeness of shapes or paths—for instance, distinguishing between the shortest path edges and others in a logistics graph, as demonstrated in their results. This approach effectively injects geometric and topological priors into the learning process, enhancing the model's ability to generalize and interpret complex spatial layouts.

Furthermore, the confluence of GNNs and attention mechanics has birthed the subfield of Graph Transformers. While standard GNNs are limited by their local neighborhood aggregation, Transformers excel at capturing global context via self-attention. Combining these allows for models that respect graph structure while accessing global dependencies. The survey <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision: A Task-Oriented Perspective> notes that Graph Transformers are becoming increasingly prevalent in analyzing 3D data, such as point clouds and meshes, where local geometry and global shape must be understood simultaneously. The  framework further advances this by introducing "Simplicial Attention Pooling" (SAP), which uses self-attention and cross-attention to coarsen the simplicial complex while preserving critical topological information. This is a vital development because standard graph pooling often struggles to maintain structural integrity during downsampling. By integrating these mechanisms, the model becomes more robust to the "random graph" construction problems often cited in earlier GNN literature, offering a mathematically grounded approach to relational learning.

The efficacy of these hybrid architectures is not limited to general computer vision but has profound implications for specialized, high-stakes domains like medical imaging and agriculture, where interpretability and robustness are paramount. As noted in <The Role of Deep Learning in Advancing Computer Vision Applications: A Comprehensive Systematic Review>, interpretability remains a major barrier to the clinical deployment of AI. Hybrid models that combine CNNs (for visualization like Grad-CAM) with GNNs (for relational reasoning) offer a path forward. In <MedicoCNN-GNN>, the use of SHAP and Grad-CAM alongside the GNN component allows clinicians to verify not just *where* the model is looking, but *how* it relates different features to make a diagnosis. Similarly, in <ECS-tea: a bio-inspired high-precision detection and localization algorithm for young shoots of Pu-erh tea>, hybrid attention mechanisms (Spatial-Channel Synergistic Attention) are used to detect small targets in cluttered, natural environments. The success of these hybrid models across such diverse applications—from <Poisonous Mushroom Detection Using Graph Neural Networks> to <Crop Yield Prediction Using Multimodal Meta-Transformer and Temporal Graph Neural Networks>—demonstrates a unifying theme: pure convolutional backbones struggle with data uncertainty and complex relational structures, whereas hybrid models leverage attention for focus and GNNs for context.

However, the increasing complexity of hybrid architectures introduces new challenges, most notably regarding adversarial robustness and computational efficiency. While models like  or MedicoCNN-GNN offer superior feature extraction, they may expand the attack surface for adversarial perturbations. <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey> highlights that as models become more sophisticated, so too must our understanding of their vulnerabilities. Interestingly, the structural reasoning provided by GNNs might offer an inherent defense against certain types of digital perturbations that fail to respect the underlying topology of the data. Conversely, the reliance on attention mechanisms makes these models potentially susceptible to patch-based attacks that exploit the "blind spots" of the attention head. Furthermore, the computational cost of calculating attention matrices over high-resolution feature maps or graph nodes remains a bottleneck. The polynomial approximation techniques introduced in  to approximate Hodge-Laplacian filters are a step toward addressing this, but scaling these hybrid models to real-time applications like autonomous driving (as seen in <Deep learning for object detection and scene perception in self-driving cars>) requires continued optimization. The trade-off between the enhanced expressiveness of hybrid models and their computational footprint is a central theme in current research, driving the development of lightweight hybrids and efficient attention approximations.

In conclusion, the trajectory of modern computer vision is moving away from monolithic, single-architecture solutions toward modular, hybrid systems that combine convolutional processing, graph-based relational reasoning, and attention-based dynamic weighting. By synthesizing the local feature extraction of CNNs with the global structural awareness of GNNs and the contextual focus of attention mechanisms, these models address the core limitations of their predecessors. They provide superior performance on complex tasks, offer enhanced interpretability through structural visualization, and demonstrate greater robustness in handling diverse data modalities. As evidenced by the advancements in <MedicoCNN-GNN> and , the future lies in architectures that are not just deep, but topologically aware and contextually focused, bridging the gap between data-driven learning and geometric reasoning.

#### Transformer Vulnerabilities and Robustness

The emergence of Vision Transformers (ViTs) marked a pivotal shift in the landscape of computer vision, moving away from the spatially inductive biases of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) toward architectures reliant solely on self-attention mechanisms. Initially hailed for their improved scalability and performance on large-scale datasets, ViTs have subsequently drawn intense scrutiny regarding their robustness properties. Surveys such as <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses> have dedicated specific sections to this architectural transition, analyzing whether the global receptive field of transformers offers inherent protection against adversarial perturbations compared to the local receptive fields of CNNs. The consensus emerging from recent literature presents a counterintuitive and nuanced picture: while ViTs often demonstrate superior resilience to common corruptions (such as blur or weather effects) due to their texture-biased learning versus the shape-biased learning of CNNs, they remain highly susceptible to, and in some cases even more vulnerable than CNNs to, sophisticated adversarial attacks. This vulnerability stems largely from the mechanism of tokenization, which slices images into patches, creating a unique attack surface where perturbations can be localized to effect global misclassification.

A critical insight highlighted in <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses> is that the standard adversarial robustness benchmarks may not fully capture the distinct failure modes of ViTs. While early conjectures suggested that the "patch-based" nature of ViTs might make them robust to localized attacks, evidence suggests the opposite. Specialized attacks like  specifically exploit the self-attention mechanism to identify and manipulate the most influential tokens, effectively demonstrating that *patch-based attacks on transformer tokens* are a potent threat vector. Unlike CNNs, where perturbations are often optimized over the continuous pixel space, attacks on ViTs can enforce discrete constraints that correspond to actual image patches, potentially leading to more physically realizable threats. This distinction challenges the community to move beyond simple $L_p$ norm metrics (like $L_\infty$) and evaluate robustness against adversarial patches that directly interact with the token embedding process. The vulnerability is exacerbated by the fact that self-attention allows for long-range dependencies; a small perturbation in a seemingly unimportant patch can have outsized effects if the attention mechanism assigns it a high weight for the classification task.

The question of whether ViTs are inherently more robust than CNNs remains a subject of debate and rigorous empirical testing. Some studies referenced in the literature suggest that label smoothing and higher weight decay during pre-training can improve ViT robustness, yet they generally lag behind specialized adversarial training baselines on standard benchmarks like <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>. However, the architectural difference is significant. CNNs rely on fixed kernels that process local neighborhoods, whereas ViTs aggregate global information. This global aggregation theoretically allows ViTs to discern holistic patterns that might be harder to perturb, yet the optimization landscape of the attention map is highly non-convex and sensitive. The susceptibility of ViTs to adversarial examples reveals that the "bag of tricks" needed to make CNNs robust—such as weight averaging and specific optimization routines—does not automatically transfer to ViTs. Consequently, the threat model for ViTs requires a re-evaluation of standard attack strategies, moving toward gradient-based attacks that respect the discrete nature of patches or those that specifically target the query-key-value projections within the transformer blocks.

In response to these vulnerabilities, the research community is increasingly looking toward *certified defenses* as a pathway to provable robustness for ViTs. Standard empirical defenses like Adversarial Training, the current dominant strategy discussed in <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses>, introduce a significant computational overhead for ViTs because generating adversarial examples requires expensive forward and backward passes through the deep architecture. Certified defenses, conversely, offer mathematical guarantees that no perturbation within a specific radius can alter the model's prediction. While certified defenses have been explored for CNNs using randomized smoothing or interval bound propagation, applying these to ViTs is computationally expensive due to the quadratic complexity of attention. However, the structure of transformers actually lends itself surprisingly well to these formal verification methods if one can bound the attention maps. Emerging work focuses on extending these guarantees to the patch-based nature of ViTs, ensuring that even if an adversary modifies a specific set of patches (tokens), the aggregate attention score remains below the decision threshold for misclassification.

Furthermore, the intersection of relational reasoning and graph-based learning offers a promising avenue for enhancing the robustness of transformer architectures. By framing the ViT not just as a sequence of patches but as a graph structure—where patches are nodes and attention weights are edges—defenses can leverage techniques discussed in <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision>. The self-attention mechanism in ViTs is topologically similar to the message-passing mechanisms in Graph Attention Networks (GATs). This similarity suggests that robustness techniques developed for Graph Neural Networks, such as  which utilizes Hodge-Laplacian operators to enforce higher-order topological constraints, could be adapted to ViTs. By enforcing that the attention graph adheres to specific topological properties (e.g., smoothness or sparsity), one might mitigate the adversarial manipulation of attention maps. This approach moves beyond standard adversarial training, offering a structural regularizer that penalizes adversarial attention distributions, thereby aligning the vulnerability analysis of ViTs with the broader regime of relational reasoning and graph topology.

Finally, the specific challenges of patch-based attacks on transformer tokens necessitate a paradigm shift in evaluation metrics. The field is moving away from relying solely on clean accuracy and PGD-based robust accuracy. As noted in the critical analysis within <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses>, the "Auto-Attack" framework has become the de facto standard for evaluating defenses, as it prevents "obfuscated gradients." Applying Auto-Attack to ViTs often reveals a stark drop in robustness compared to CNNs, specifically concerning spatially constrained attacks. The "patch-based" reality means that an adversary does not need to perturb every pixel; they only need to compromise a single, critical token or a small group of tokens. This localized vulnerability contrasts with the distributed vulnerability assumed by $L_\infty$ attacks. Consequently, future work must decouple the evaluation of clean performance from the evaluation of patch robustness, perhaps utilizing datasets beyond CIFAR-10, such as ImageNet, where the spatial arrangement of objects and the distinctiveness of patches play a much larger role in the classification confidence of ViTs. The architectural shift from CNN to ViT has thus exposed a complex trade-off between global information integration and localized adversarial susceptibility, requiring a new generation of both attacks and defenses tailored to the token-based structure of modern vision models.


Adversarial robustness, widely understood as a fundamental vulnerability inherent to deep learning models, has catalyzed a paradigm shift from empirical "arms race" contributions toward defenses offering more rigorous, mathematical guarantees. This transition reflects the limitations of obfuscated gradients and heuristic countermeasures, which frequently fail under adaptive attacks. In response, the defensive landscape has bifurcated into two dominant methodologies: **empirical defenses**, which prioritize performance against known threat models, and **certified defenses**, which seek to provide formal guarantees of stability against bounded perturbations. As detailed in the influential work by Madry et al., robust optimization against a worst-case adversary serves as the theoretical bedrock for empirical methods, specifically validating Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) as a universal first-order adversary. However, the field is simultaneously moving beyond this min-max paradigm toward conceptual frameworks for inherent robustness, which investigate the root causes of vulnerability such as high dimensionality and the quality of the learned data manifold.

This section synthesizes the evolution of these defensive strategies. It begins by establishing the dominance of **Empirical Defenses**, emphasizing adversarial training as the baseline standard while acknowledging the significant trade-offs regarding clean accuracy and computational efficiency. Critically, these empirical approaches are scrutinized for the "obfuscated gradients" phenomenon, a failure mode where defenses appear robust because they hinder gradient-based evaluation rather than genuinely resisting attacks. To contrast this probabilistic security, the discussion shifts to **Certified Defenses**, which utilize techniques like randomized smoothing and interval bound propagation to quantify robustness. Here, the focus is on the theoretical necessity of formal guarantees and the practical challenge of scaling these methods to large-scale vision tasks. Finally, the section highlights a growing trend toward **Conceptual Frameworks for Inherent Robustness**, representing a departure from reactive patching. These frameworks propose holistic architectural designs—often utilizing generative models or structural regularization—to address the fundamental brittleness of neural networks, linking the validity of defensive measures to the geometry of the data manifold itself.

#### Empirical Defenses: Training and Detection

Empirical defenses in adversarial machine learning primarily encompass strategies that harden models against adversarial attacks without providing formal, mathematical guarantees of robustness. Unlike certified defenses, which focus on verifiable bounds within specific perturbation norms, empirical methods like Adversarial Training, ensemble strategies, and detection mechanisms rely on heuristic improvements that prove effective against a wide range of existing threats in practice. The foundational work <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks> reframes this challenge through a min-max optimization lens, positing that robust training is essentially solving for model parameters that minimize the loss incurred by a worst-case adversary. This principled perspective validated Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) as a universal first-order adversary, suggesting that training against a strong, multi-step PGD adversary yields a model that resists a broad spectrum of gradient-based attacks. This approach has become the de facto standard for robust training, often referred to as PGD-based Adversarial Training (AT).

However, the widespread adoption of Adversarial Training reveals a significant trade-off: the cost of robustness is often a depression in clean accuracy. While models trained with PGD become significantly more resilient, they typically exhibit a drop in performance on unperturbed, benign data. This phenomenon has sparked a dual-track investigation into pure robustness versus practical utility. In the survey <A Survey of Adversarial Examples in Computer Vision: Attack, Defense, and Beyond>, the authors highlight this "robustness vs. accuracy" trade-off as a central open problem, noting that while adversarial training is currently the most effective empirical defense, it does not fully address the underlying fragility of the feature space. Similarly, the survey <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> benchmarks state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods and observes that adversarial training dominates the landscape, yet limitations remain in scalability.

This trade-off drives the exploration of ensemble methods as a mechanism to diversify decision boundaries without sacrificing excessive clean performance. In <Ensemble Methods as a Defense to Adversarial Perturbations Against Deep Neural Networks>, the authors empirically demonstrate that ensembles composed of independently trained models—whether via random initialization, different architectures, or bagging—create a fragmented landscape that is hostile to adversarial propagation. A key insight from this work is that an adversarial example crafted to fool one specific model in the ensemble is significantly less likely to transfer to others. Specifically, when evaluating against strong iterative attacks like the Basic Iterative Method (BIM), the study found that ensembles utilizing Gaussian Noise augmentation or Bagging retained exceptionally high accuracy (e.g., ~98% on MNIST against BIM) compared to single models, which saw accuracy plummet to single digits. The mechanism at play here is the averaging of gradients; although theoretically, an attacker with white-box access could compute the average gradient across the ensemble to find a universal perturbation, the divergence in individual model gradients creates interference that stabilizes the ensemble decision. This suggests that ensemble methods effectively "smoothen" the loss landscape in a way that complicates gradient-based optimization for the attacker.

However, the efficacy of adversarial training and ensembles against first-order attacks has led to a reliance on gradient-based evaluation. This reliance masks a critical vulnerability: the "obfuscated gradients" problem, where defenses appear robust because they break the gradient flow used by standard attacks, rather than actually resisting the attack. The survey <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> explicitly critiques this, noting that many defenses fail against adaptive attacks tailored to bypass them. This brings to light the necessity of rigorous evaluation metrics, such as Auto-Attack, which aggregate diverse attack strategies to uncover hidden weaknesses. Moving beyond the assumption that gradient masking equals robustness is essential for the field to mature.

When robust training falls short, detection strategies offer an alternative line of defense, aiming to identify and reject adversarial inputs rather than correcting the model's behavior. A notable advancement in this area is presented in <Detecting Adversaries, yet Faltering to Noise? Leveraging Conditional Variational AutoEncoders for Adversary Detection in the Presence of Noisy Images>. This work introduces a detection pipeline based on Conditional Variational AutoEncoders (CVAEs), leveraging the intuition that adversarial perturbations displace an image from the probability distribution of its (incorrectly) predicted class. By training a separate CVAE for each class on clean data, the detector computes the reconstruction error when an adversarial image is passed through the CVAE conditioned on the target model's predicted label. Since the adversarial image lies in a low-density region of the wrong class's distribution, the reconstruction error is anomalously high. Crucially, this method distinguishes between malicious adversarial perturbations and benign random noise; random noise typically preserves the semantic features allowing for reasonable reconstruction, whereas adversarial noise specifically maximizes confusion. This capability to differentiate noise from adversaries addresses a common blind spot in detection literature.

While the CVAE approach relies on distributional mismatch, other detection strategies and training modifications seek to alter the feature extraction process directly. A Comparative Study on Adversarial Attacks and Defense Mechanisms underscores the variety of empirical countermeasures, noting that some defenses focus on normalizing inputs or using secondary verification systems. For instance, <Adversarial-Aware Deep Learning System Based on a Secondary Classical Machine Learning Verification Approach> proposes a hybrid system where a classical machine learning model (e.g., Random Forest) verifies the output of a Deep Neural Network. The hypothesis here is that classical models, lacking the specific architectural vulnerabilities of DNNs (such as the reliance on local gradient continuity), are immune to standard adversarial attacks. This cross-checking mechanism effectively acts as a detector, flagging instances where the deep model predicts one class but the classical model strongly disagrees. This highlights a trend toward "defense in depth," where multiple heterogeneous models operate in concert.

Furthermore, deep learning vulnerabilities are not limited to global, imperceptible perturbations. Research has uncovered severe susceptibilities to localized, sparse perturbations. The paper <Simple Black-Box Adversarial Perturbations for Deep Networks> demonstrates that attacking a single pixel or a small set of pixels can yield misclassification with non-trivial probability, challenging the notion that perturbations must be dense to be effective. This is complemented by the <Sliding Window Gradient Sign Attack>, which utilizes a localized gradient sign approach to identify specific, highly vulnerable regions of an image, such as the eyes or nose in facial recognition systems. The implication is that many DNNs rely heavily on localized features, and robust defenses must account for these "deadly" regions.

In an effort to move away from empirical observation toward theoretical understanding, geometric interpretations of robustness have emerged. <A Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks> proposes the Adversarial Converging Time Score (ACTS), an attack-dependent metric that measures the time required for an optimization process to converge to an adversarial sample. This metric captures the local curvature of the loss landscape; a "flatter" landscape takes longer to traverse, implying higher robustness. This offers an alternative to computationally expensive metrics like CLEVER and provides a geometric intuition: robust models should have loss landscapes that are difficult for optimizers to navigate quickly.

The landscape of empirical defenses is further complicated by the emergence of Vision Transformers (ViTs) and alternative architectures. While originally believed to be more robust due to their global attention mechanisms, recent analysis suggests they are equally vulnerable to tailored attacks. The survey <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses> notes that specific attacks like "Patch-fool" exploit the tokenization process of ViTs, turning their architectural uniqueness into a liability. Consequently, empirical defenses for ViTs often require architectural modifications or specialized adversarial training regimes distinct from those used for CNNs.

This confluence of observations—from the dominance of PGD-based training and the diversification benefits of ensembles to the nuanced differentiation capabilities of CVAE detectors—paints a complex picture of the field. The work <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks> provides the theoretical backbone for why adversarial training works (conceptualizing it as robust optimization), but <Ensemble Methods as a Defense to Adversarial Perturbations Against Deep Neural Networks> demonstrates that diversity in model initialization or training noise provides a complementary boost. Meanwhile, detection strategies like those in <Detecting Adversaries, yet Faltering to Noise? Leveraging Conditional Variational AutoEncoders for Adversary Detection in the Presence of Noisy Images> address the limitations of training-based defenses by handling the "noise vs. adversary" ambiguity. Collectively, these staples of the literature define empirical robustness not as a single solution, but as a trade-off space involving the balancing of clean accuracy, computational cost, and the specific threat model (e.g., sparse vs. dense, digital vs. physical). The transition from simple gradient-based attacks to those exploiting localized features (<Sliding Window Gradient Sign Attack>) and the incentive for adaptive evaluation (<Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey>) signals that empirical defenses must continuously evolve beyond static training routines to encompass detection, architectural diversity, and geometric resilience.

#### Input Pre-processing and Purification

Input pre-processing and purification serves as a critical first line of defense in the adversarial robustness landscape, operating under the premise that adversaries can be neutralized by transforming inputs into their "clean" representations before they reach the primary classification model. This defensive paradigm is fundamentally rooted in the observation that adversarial perturbations, while often imperceptible to human observers, frequently reside in specific frequency bands or high-dimensional subspaces that are distinct from the semantic features learned by Deep Neural Networks (DNNs). By applying transformations such as compression, resolution scaling, or denoising, these techniques aim to statistically or geometrically map adversarial inputs back onto the manifold of legitimate data distributions. Despite the intuitive appeal of this approach, a rigorous analysis reveals a persistent and evolving "arms race" where defensive transformations are systematically dismantled by adaptive attacks designed specifically to bypass them.

A foundational strategy within this domain is **feature squeezing**, which reduces the dimensionality of the input space to eliminate high-frequency noise. The core hypothesis is that adversarial examples rely on fine-grained pixel manipulations that are destroyed when the image is quantized or smoothed. Techniques in this vein include JPEG compression, which discards high-frequency information often exploited by attacks like <Fast Gradient Sign Method> (FGSM), and bit-depth reduction. However, the efficacy of these methods is heavily contingent on the attacker's knowledge. As established in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey>, early defenses relying on input transformations were quickly exposed by adaptive adversaries who optimized their perturbations to survive the specific transformation pipeline. For instance, an attacker aware of a JPEG compression step can simply minimize the loss function on the *compressed* version of the image, resulting in perturbations that remain effective post-compression. This underscores the fragility of "obfuscated gradients"—a phenomenon where a defense appears robust because the attacker fails to account for the differentiable approximation of the transformation.

Moving beyond simple spatial operations, **autoencoder-based purification** attempts to learn a generative mapping that reconstructs a corrupted input into a clean version. While generic convolutional autoencoders have been proposed for this purpose, they often struggle to distinguish between adversarial noise and legitimate high-frequency details, leading to image blurring. A more sophisticated evolution of this approach is found in <Detecting Adversaries, yet Faltering to Noise? Leveraging Conditional Variational AutoEncoders for Adversary Detection in the Presence of Noisy Images>, which introduces a Conditional Variational AutoEncoder (CVAE) conditioned on the classifier's predicted label. This methodology leverages the intuition that a clean image of a specific class lies within a high-probability region of that class's learned distribution. Consequently, if an adversarial example causes a misclassification, it will naturally fall outside the distribution of that wrong class's data manifold. When processed by the CVAE trained on that specific (wrong) class, the reconstruction error will be anomalously high, flagging the input as adversarial. This paper demonstrates that while the CVAE effectively purifies the image in a latent sense, the underlying mechanism is also a potent detector. It highlights a crucial distinction: purification methods often serve a dual role, yet they remain susceptible to adaptive attacks that optimize perturbations to minimize the reconstruction error of the "purifier," thereby rendering the input "admissible" to the classifier while maintaining its malicious intent. This dynamic is further complicated by computationally expensive defenses like <Defense Against Adversarial Attacks using Convolutional Auto-Encoders>, which prioritize robustness over inference speed, a trade-off often deemed prohibitive for large-scale systems.

The failure of standard pre-processing methods to provide certified guarantees has spurred the exploration of more complex, non-linear purifications. A prominent emerging direction involves **diffusion-based purification**, which utilizes pre-trained generative diffusion models to denoise inputs. Unlike linear transformations, diffusion models perform iterative refinement, theoretically removing adversarial perturbations while retaining semantic content. However, this introduces significant latency. Moreover, recent studies suggest that adaptive attacks can be devised to inject "structural noise" that aligns with the diffusion model's sampling trajectory, effectively guiding the purified output back to an adversarial state. This illustrates that without a mathematical bound on the purification process, empirically robust methods inevitably face degradation under adaptive threat models.

Furthermore, the application of purifiers faces distinct challenges in **non-standard modalities**, such as infrared images. As evidenced by <Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems>, standard pre-processing techniques tuned for RGB images may not transfer effectively to thermal signatures. The inherent noise profiles and texture differences in红外数据 necessitate domain-specific purification strategies. The paper indicates that while adversarial training remains effective, the defense structure established in RGB domains requires careful adaptation to ensure that the "purification" does not inadvertently remove the vital thermal contrast required for human detection.

Finally, it is imperative to recognize that input purification exists in tension with **model-centric robustness**. Strategies such as ensemble learning, as explored in <Ensemble Methods as a Defense to Adversarial Perturbations Against Deep Neural Networks>, function as a form of "decision purification" rather than input purification. By aggregating predictions across differently initialized models, the ensemble smooths the prediction landscape, making it harder for a singular perturbation to cross decision boundaries consistently. This suggests that purification might not strictly need to happen at the input level; rather, robustifying the model's aggregation logic offers a complementary pathway. However, the survey in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> concludes that while pre-processing and purification offer a computationally cheap layer of defense, they rarely offer the unconditional safety guarantees provided by certified defenses. The "purification" process itself often acts as a differentiable layer that attackers can exploit, meaning the safety of these methods is defined not by the transformation itself, but by the robustness of the transformation's hyperparameters against a determined, adaptive adversary.

#### Certified Defenses and Formal Guarantees

The paradigm of adversarial robustness has fundamentally shifted in recent years from merely proposing empirical defenses to establishing rigorous, mathematically grounded guarantees. This transition reflects a growing maturity in the field, where the "security through obscurity" provided by obfuscated gradients is no longer deemed acceptable. Instead, researchers are focusing on certified defenses—mechanisms that provide provable lower bounds on the robustness of a model against any perturbation within a specified budget. While empirical defenses like adversarial training have demonstrated significant efficacy in the "arms race" against attacks, their security remains probabilistic and dependent on the specific strong adversaries used during training. Consequently, the pursuit of formal guarantees has emerged as the gold standard for deploying deep learning in safety-critical applications. The foundational argument for this shift, extensively detailed in <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>, is that robustness should be framed as a min-max optimization problem. This work established that training against a strong, first-order adversary like Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) yields models with reliably high robustness against a wide spectrum of attacks. However, even this state-of-the-art adversarial training does not offer a proof that *no* adversarial example exists. This gap motivates the drive toward certified defenses, which aim to bound the model's output space to ensure that within a certain perturbation radius, the classification remains invariant.

A major barrier to the widespread adoption of formal verification techniques, such as Interval Bound Propagation (IBP) and CROWN-IBP, is their computational prohibitions, particularly on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. To bridge the gap between theoretical verification and practical evaluation, the community has sought scalable metrics that approximate worst-case behavior without the cost of complete verification. This need led to the development of metrics like CLEVER (Cross Lipschitz Extreme Value for nEtwork Robustness), which provides a lower bound on the robustness of a model by estimating the local Lipschitz constant. However, CLEVER relies on high-dimensional Monte Carlo sampling of the input space, making it computationally expensive and sensitive to the number of samples. Addressing these limitations, <A Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks> introduces the **Adversarial Converging Time Score (ACTS)**. ACTS moves beyond abstract bounds to leverage the geometry of the output manifold. It posits that "robustness" can be quantified by the "time" required for an adversarial perturbation to traverse the distance between the logit of the true class and the most vulnerable target class. By approximating the speed of convergence using the Data Jacobian Matrix (DJM), ACTS provides an instance-specific metric that is orders of magnitude faster than CLEVER and demonstrates superior empirical performance in separating robust from vulnerable samples.

While inference-time metrics and robust training paradigms improve resilience, the ultimate goal for critical systems is a mathematically provable certificate. Randomized Smoothing has risen as a leading technique for obtaining such certificates, specifically for $L_2$ robustness. This method involves creating a "smoothed" classifier by classifying a random augmentation of the input; statistically, one can then derive a confidence interval that guarantees the prediction remains constant under any perturbation smaller than a calculated radius. This approach offers a distinct advantage in terms of scalability and model agnosticism, allowing standard architectures to be certified. However, it introduces a trade-off between certification radius and clean accuracy. In parallel with randomized smoothing, research into deterministic bounds continues, focusing on propagating intervals through the network layers (IBP). While IBP can theoretically provide tighter bounds, training models that are stable under interval propagation remains a challenge. Together, these methods represent the two main pillars of certified defenses: statistical certificates via randomization and deterministic certificates via bound propagation. The shift towards these methods signifies a move away from ad-hoc input transformations—which often fail under adaptive attacks—and toward robustifying the model's intrinsic geometry.

The complexity of certified robustness is further compounded by the diverse threat landscape, extending beyond simple $L_p$ norm constraints to physical world perturbations. As discussed in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey>, the rise of physical attacks, such as adversarial patches <Adversarial Patch Attacks and Defences in Vision-Based Tasks: A Survey> and optical perturbations, demands a re-evaluation of what constitutes a "valid" certificate. Certified defenses must account not just for pixel-wise noise, but for geometric transformations, lighting variations, and occlusions. This requirement catalyzed the development of frameworks like Expectation Over Transformations (EOT), which attempt to certify robustness against stochastic physical transformations. However, the stochastic nature of the physical world often clashes with the determinism of formal verification. Consequently, the field is seeing a convergence where certified defenses are often evaluated in simulation, while physical robustness still largely relies on empirical adversarial training. The survey <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey> further argues that evaluating physical attacks requires a multi-faceted metric (hiPAA) that considers stealth, aesthetics, and economics, suggesting that purely mathematical certificates are insufficient for real-world deployment. They posit that a robust system must anticipate the "adversarial medium"—the physical carrier of the perturbation—which requires a holistic system-level certification rather than just a pixel-level one.

Significant challenges persist in the domain of certified defenses, primarily regarding the trade-off between robustness and generalization. It is well-documented that robust models often require significantly higher capacity than standard classifiers <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>. Furthermore, there is a tension between "provable" robustness and "effective" robustness. A model might have a high certified radius on average, but fail to classify specific, structurally important inputs correctly. The validation of these metrics also remains contentious; the <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> paper highlights the problem of "obfuscated gradients," where defenses that appear robust actually break the gradient signals required for evaluation, leading to false confidence. This skepticism has led to an industry standard where defenses must withstand adaptive attacks designed specifically to break them. For instance, certified defenses based on input preprocessing often fail when the attack is aware of the preprocessing step. Therefore, the community increasingly views Pre-Activation Certified Universality (PACE) and randomized smoothing not as post-hoc fixes, but as architectural constraints that must be baked into the training loop.

Another nuance is the distinction between "instance-specific" and "global" robustness. Metrics like ACTS provide a granular view of robustness, identifying specific inputs where the model is "close" to the decision boundary. This is invaluable for interpretability and debugging, offering a safer, faster alternative to exhaustive verification for deployment screening. However, global guarantees are required for certification. The synthesis of these approaches—using fast, geometric insights to inform robust training loops that yield high-probability certificates—is the current frontier. As noted in the theoretical analysis of adversarial phenomena <Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review>, the underlying geometry of the manifold (linear vs. non-linear paths) dictates the difficulty of finding adversarial examples. Certified defenses essentially attempt to "smooth" this manifold or expand the decision boundary regions to ensure that no adversarial path exists.

Looking ahead, the integration of graph-based learning and topological reasoning presents a new horizon for robustness. Recent works in <Graph-based Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes> and  demonstrate that incorporating higher-order structural information (nodes, edges, triangles) can enhance robustness. In the context of certification, this implies that robustness might not just be a property of the pixel space, but of the relational space. If a model understands that a "traffic light" must be connected to a "road" topology, it becomes inherently more robust to pixel-level perturbations that do not alter these semantic relationships. This shift from local pixel certification to global topological certification represents a promising direction for making AI safe for high-stakes applications like autonomous driving and medical diagnosis.

In summary, the field of certified defenses is rapidly evolving from a theoretical curiosity to a practical necessity. While methods like IBP and randomized smoothing provide the mathematical machinery for guarantees, they are often hampered by computational costs and tight integration requirements with specific loss functions. The development of efficient, attack-agnostic metrics like ACTS facilitates the rapid iteration of models before full certification, addressing the bottleneck of scalability. However, as physical and black-box attacks become more sophisticated, the definition of "robustness" must expand. The current consensus, synthesized from broad surveys and focused technical contributions <A Comprehensive Review of Adversarial Attacks and Defense Strategies in Deep Neural Networks>, is that no single method offers a silver bullet. Rather, robustness will likely be achieved through a hybrid approach: utilizing geometric insights for efficient monitoring, adversarial training for general resilience, and randomized smoothing for formal guarantees in critical subsystems. Ultimately, proving the safety of deep learning systems requires moving beyond the distinction between digital and physical, between certification and training, and integrating them into a unified framework of "defense-in-depth."

#### Conceptual Frameworks for Inherent Robustness (TPre-ADL)

In the ongoing pursuit of trustworthy artificial intelligence, the field has largely been defined by a reactive "arms race" dynamic in which researchers rapidly design patches to mitigate newly discovered threats. However, a subset of recent literature argues for a paradigm shift from reactive defense to the construction of inherently robust systems. This proactive approach seeks to address the *fundamental properties* of deep learning models and data that render them vulnerable in the first place. By synthesizing insights from adversarial machine learning, generative modeling, and ensemble methods, these frameworks aim to inoculate models against a broad spectrum of attack vectors rather than fortifying against specific incursions.

The foundational motivation for this shift is rooted in the systematic analysis of model vulnerabilities. Survey works such as *Enhancing trustworthy deep learning for image classification against evasion attacks: a systematic literature review* and *A Comprehensive Review of Adversarial Attacks and Defense Strategies in Deep Neural Networks* argue that simply hardening a classifier is insufficient if the root causes of brittleness remain unaddressed. These analyses identify core weaknesses, including the exploitation of high-dimensional data distributions, the existence of low-probability adversarial subspaces, and the reliance on non-robust features that are susceptible to imperceptible perturbations (e.g., *A Survey of Adversarial Examples in Computer Vision: Attack, Defense, and Beyond*). The recognition of these systemic flaws has catalyzed the development of frameworks that treat robustness not as a metric to be maximized on a held-out test set, but as a structural attribute of the model itself. This represents a maturation of the field, moving beyond the "patching" mentality toward a holistic redesign of learning pipelines.

At the forefront of this structural reimagining is the conceptual framework proposed in the seminal study, **"Enhancing trustworthy deep learning for image classification against evasion attacks: a systematic literature review"**. This work introduces the *Transferable Pre-trained Adversarial Deep Learning (TPre-ADL)* framework, which explicitly leverages Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to detect and neutralize the characteristics innate to adversarial samples. Rather than training a detection network on a union of clean and adversarial data samples, TPre-ADL utilizes a pre-trained GAN to learn the manifold of "normal" data. In this architecture, the generator is repurposed to act as a robust feature extractor and dimensionality reducer, transforming high-dimensional inputs into a stable latent space. This directly mitigates the vulnerability of *high dimensionality*, which adversaries often exploit by navigating the sparse corners of the feature space where models tend to extrapolate linearly (*Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks*). Simultaneously, the discriminator is deployed as an anomaly detector, flagging inputs that statistically deviate from the training distribution. This mechanism creates a "safety buffer" around the decision boundary, effectively defending against *non-robust features* without requiring explicit generation of adversarial examples for training data augmentation (*Feature autoencoder for detecting adversarial examples*).

The efficacy of this GAN-centric approach is further contextualized by the broader utility of generative models in understanding and attacking deep neural networks. Research into *Generative Adversarial Attacks* highlights a key trade-off: while GANs can generate highly transferable adversarial instances from pure noise, they are also capable of synthesizing realistic perturbations starting from real images. Frameworks like TPre-ADL turn this attack vector into a defensive weapon by using the learned generative manifold as a "healthy" baseline. If an input cannot be effectively encoded or is rejected by the discriminator, it is inferred to be adversarial. This is distinct from *Defense Against Adversarial Attacks using Convolutional Auto-Encoders*, which focuses solely on reconstructing images to "purify" them. Instead, TPre-ADL’s innovation lies in its dual-use of GAN components: the generator to stabilize the feature extraction process and the discriminator to validate input integrity. This architectural choice addresses the theoretical finding that deep learning models often violate the smoothness assumption—a fundamental requirement for stability—by enforcing a consistency based on the learned data distribution (*Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision? Adversarial Examples Against the iCub Humanoid*).

A parallel strategy for enhancing inherent robustness, which can be effectively integrated with the GAN-based pipeline, is the use of **ensemble methods**. As empirically demonstrated in **"Ensemble Methods as a Defense to Adversarial Perturbations Against Deep Neural Networks"**, diversity among models is a powerful deterrent against adversarial attacks. The study shows that an adversarial perturbation crafted to fool a single model (even via the average gradient across the ensemble) is statistically unlikely to deceive a diverse set of models simultaneously. This is particularly relevant when considering the "high dimensionality" and "linear regions" vulnerabilities. A single model may map a specific adversarial input to a confident, incorrect classification because it views the input through a fixed set of non-robust feature detectors. However, a bagging ensemble creates multiple, slightly shifted decision boundaries. The experimental results on datasets like MNIST and CIFAR-10 reveal that ensembles—specifically those augmented with Gaussian noise or using bagging—do not just trade accuracy for robustness; they often improve both. This suggests that the aggregation of weak learners mitigates the over-reliance on specific brittle features. By diversifying the ensemble’s assumptions about the data manifold, these methods provide a probabilistic defense that complements the deterministic checks of a TPre-ADL-like architecture.

Furthermore, inherent robustness is not limited to GANs or static ensembles but extends to the dynamic adaptation of **Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)** and hybrid architectures. In complex vision tasks, such as medical imaging or autonomous driving, simple pixel-level robustness is insufficient. Spatial and relational dependencies must be preserved. The study **"Optimizing Federated Graph Learning with Inherent Structural Knowledge and Dual-Densely Connected GNNs"** argues for capturing "inherent structural knowledge" to combat the non-IID (non-Independent and Identically Distributed) nature of data, which is a precursor to adversarial susceptibility. By explicitly encoding structural information (topology) alongside features, models can leverage relationships between entities to verify consistency, making them more resistant to noise. Similarly, the hybrid model proposed in **"MedicoCNN-GNN: A Hybrid Model with Integrated Attention Mechanism for Medical Image Classification"** integrates CNNs, GNNs, and attention mechanisms to enhance multi-class classification. This approach ensures that robustness is built in at the feature-extraction level (CNN), relationship-modeling level (GNN), and focus-optimization level (Attention). This multi-layered defense strategy aligns with the TPre-ADL philosophy: robustness is an emergent property of a well-structured architecture that respects the topology of the data, rather than a post-hoc correction.

However, the transition to these advanced, inherent robustness frameworks is not without significant challenges. The authors of the TPre-ADL framework explicitly acknowledge limitations regarding the diversity and bias of the GAN training data; if the generative model fails to capture the full spectrum of "normal" data, the discriminator may fail to recognize sophisticated adversarial samples that still lie reasonably close to the expected manifold. Furthermore, the computational cost is a major barrier. Training a GAN, maintaining an ensemble, or performing graph-based reasoning requires substantial resources, raising questions about the scalability of these methods for real-time, resource-constrained environments (*A Survey of the Recent Architectures of Deep Convolutional Neural Networks*). Additionally, the assumption that a well-learned data distribution equates to security has been challenged by more sophisticated attacks. The arms race continues: while TPre-ADL defends against statistical anomalies, an adaptive adversary aware of the discriminator’s logic might craft perturbations that remain indistinguishable from the learned distribution—a concept explored in *Enhanced Attacks on Defensively Distilled Deep Neural Networks*.

Consequently, a comprehensive framework for inherent robustness must also integrate mechanisms for **interpretability and verification**. As argued in *Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review*, understanding *why* a model is robust is crucial for trusting it. The proposed "future directions" of TPre-ADL include the integration of Explainable AI (XAI) to verify that the features being utilized are semantically meaningful and robust. This aligns with the "uncertainty-aware" approaches emerging in the field, such as **"FruitProm: Probabilistic Maturity Estimation and Detection of Fruits and Vegetables"**, which reframes classification as a continuous, probabilistic learning task. By explicitly modeling uncertainty, models can "reject" classification when inputs fall into adversarial regions. This "classification with reject option" strategy (*Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision?*) is a vital component of inherent robustness; it allows the system to recognize the limits of its knowledge, rather than confidently predicting on adversarial inputs.

In summary, the development of conceptual frameworks like TPre-ADL marks a critical evolution in the pursuit of trustworthy AI. By moving beyond reactive retraining and adversarial patching, these methods utilize GANs for feature stabilization and anomaly detection, leverage ensemble diversity to smooth decision boundaries, and incorporate structural reasoning via GNNs. They address the root causes of vulnerability—high dimensionality, non-robust features, and linear extrapolations—rather than just the symptoms. Nevertheless, the realization of these frameworks depends on overcoming substantial hurdles in computational efficiency, data diversity, and adaptive resistance. As the field progresses, the synthesis of robust architectural design (e.g., TPre-ADL, hybrid CNN-GNNs) with rigorous evaluation standards (e.g., hiPAA for physical attacks) and interpretability metrics will be essential. Only by building systems that are intrinsically aligned with the structural properties of the data can we hope to close the gap between current deep learning performance and the ultimate goal of secure, deployable, and trustworthy AI.


The transition of computer vision from generalized benchmarks to deeply specialized, high-stakes domains has exposed a critical dependency between performance and reliability. In fields such as agriculture, healthcare, and autonomous robotics, model predictions dictate physical actions and clinical diagnoses, meaning that standard accuracy metrics are insufficient; these systems require inherent robustness and interpretability. As such, the study of adversarial phenomena—where imperceptible input perturbations cause catastrophic failure—has moved beyond theoretical curiosity to a practical necessity for real-world deployment. This survey does not treat domain-specific applications merely as endpoints for existing techniques but rather as laboratories that reveal the nuanced constraints and failure modes of modern deep learning.

To organize this synthesis, we first establish the landscape of these specialized fields through the lens of foundational computer vision evolution. The progression from hand-crafted descriptors to deep learning, as outlined in <The Role of Deep Learning in Advancing Computer Vision Applications>, provides the architectural basis for these applications. However, the efficacy of architectures like CNNs and Transformers is fundamentally challenged by the brittleness discussed in <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses>. This section, therefore, explores how domain-specific constraints—ranging from the biological variability in crops to the safety-critical requirements of autonomous driving—drive a paradigm shift toward hybrid architectures and defense-in-depth strategies. By connecting the broad vulnerabilities outlined in <A Survey of Adversarial Examples in Computer Vision: Attack, Defense, and Beyond> with the distinct challenges of high-dimensional, multi-modal data in these niche fields, we argue that future robust AI relies on bridging the gap between adversarial theory and domain-specific pragmatism.

#### Agricultural Intelligence and Crop Management

The integration of deep learning into agriculture represents a paradigm shift from traditional, experience-based cultivation to precision agriculture, leveraging computational intelligence to optimize crop management. This transition is fundamentally driven by the capability of deep learning models to extract intricate patterns from vast and heterogeneous datasets, a necessity given the complex biological systems involved. The evolution of this field is closely tied to the advancements in core computer vision and machine learning architectures. Foundational literature, such as <Deep Learning: A Comprehensive Overview on Techniques, Taxonomy, Applications and Research Directions>, establishes the theoretical underpinnings of the hierarchical feature learning that distinguishes deep neural networks from classical machine learning. It provides the necessary context for understanding why deep learning surpasses traditional methods, such as those relying on manual feature extraction like Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT), in handling the high variability of natural environments. The "Deep Learning vs. Traditional Computer Vision" discourse underscores this transition, highlighting hybrid approaches that combine robust classical techniques with modern deep learning to address novel challenges like panoramic vision, thereby mirroring the current drive for robust, real-world agricultural systems. Furthermore, the trajectory of deep learning architectures, from early Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to complex hybrid models, is meticulously charted in surveys like <A State-of-the-Art Survey on Deep Learning Theory and Architectures>. These reviews illustrate how architectural innovations—such as residual connections in ResNet or multi-scale processing in Inception—have enabled the precise tasks required for crop analysis, from identification to yield prediction. Interestingly, parallels can be drawn with advancements in seemingly disparate fields; for instance, the adaptation of pre-trained models like DenseNet and Xception for fruit recognition in <DenseNet-201 and Xception Pre-Trained Deep Learning Models for Fruit Recognition> demonstrates the power of transfer learning, a concept thoroughly explored in <Review of deep learning: concepts, CNN architectures, challenges, applications, future directions>. This latter review not only details the evolution of CNN architectures but also addresses critical challenges like data scarcity, which is a significant bottleneck in agricultural contexts where large, labeled datasets are often unavailable.

The application of these technologies has matured across several key domains, most notably in crop identification and yield prediction, which are foundational to digital farming. The systematic transition from manual inspection to automated analysis is evident in work reviewed by <A Review of Computer Vision and Deep Learning Applications in Crop Growth Management>, which categorizes the application of deep learning into crop identification, grading, disease monitoring, and weed detection. This paper underscores the industry’s pivot toward digital, data-driven methodologies to address global challenges like resource scarcity and climate change. In the realm of yield prediction, achieving high accuracy often requires synergizing multiple data types. For example, <Crop Yield Prediction Using Multimodal Meta-Transformer and Temporal Graph Neural Networks> showcases a sophisticated framework that fuses weather, soil, temporal data, and plant imagery. By employing Meta-Transformers to model multimodal interactions and Temporal Graph Neural Networks to handle sequential data, this approach achieves nearly 97% classification accuracy, significantly outperforming traditional temporal models like LSTMs. This highlights the trend toward integrating diverse data streams for holistic farm intelligence. Similarly, specialized systems are emerging to address specific crops with high precision, such as <ECS-tea: a bio-inspired high-precision detection and localization algorithm for young shoots of Pu-erh tea>, which combines lightweight network design with attention mechanisms to achieve near-perfect accuracy in detecting tea buds for automated harvesting.

Beyond mere identification, the granularity of analysis has deepened to include maturity estimation, disease monitoring, and even species-level classification, including safety-critical distinctions like poisonous mushroom detection. A pivotal advancement in this area is the move from discrete classification to continuous, probabilistic modeling of biological states. The work in <FruitProm: Probabilistic Maturity Estimation and Detection of Fruits and Vegetables> challenges the standard classification paradigm for ripeness (e.g., ripe/unripe), arguing that such rigid labels conflict with the continuous nature of biological ripening. FruitProm introduces a probabilistic head to an object detector, predicting a continuous maturity distribution and crucially, an associated uncertainty score. This is a profound shift that far exceeds the capabilities of classical computer vision. In disease monitoring, the focus is often on specialized architectures. For skin disease classification, <Classification of Skin Disease Using Deep Learning Neural Networks with MobileNet V2 and LSTM> presents a hybrid model utilizing MobileNet V2 for feature extraction and LSTM for capturing stateful information, demonstrating an accuracy of over 85%. Similarly, to ensure food safety and efficient harvesting, novel algorithms like <Poisonous Mushroom Detection Using Graph Neural Networks> explore the use of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to classify edible versus poisonous mushrooms by analyzing pixel relationships as graph structures. These case studies exemplify the field's move toward highly specialized, domain-aware models that tackle unique biological constraints and operational demands.

However, the increasing reliance on deep learning in agriculture, a sector with direct implications for food security and safety, exposes these systems to the same vulnerabilities found in other computer vision domains, specifically adversarial attacks. Adversarial examples—imperceptible perturbations that cause catastrophic model failure—pose a significant threat to the reliability of automated agricultural systems. The foundational understanding of these threats is established in comprehensive surveys like <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses> and <Research Progress and Challenges on Application-Driven Adversarial Examples: A Survey>, which categorize attacks based on attacker knowledge (white-box, black-box) and goals. While most research focuses on standard vision tasks, the principles are directly applicable to agriculture. Vulnerabilities such as sensor noise or environmental changes in the field could be exploited by the same mechanisms used in digital attacks like FGSM or PGD, as detailed in <Foundational and Evolutionary Attack Methodologies>. Conversely, the field is beginning to explore robustness and defense. <Enhancing trustworthy deep learning for image classification against evasion attacks: a systematic literature review> advocates for a deeper understanding of adversarial vulnerabilities rather than just defensive patches. It proposes frameworks like the Transferable Pretrained Adversarial Deep Learning framework (TPre-ADL) to improve robustness. This concern for trustworthiness is echoed in reviews like <The Role of Deep Learning in Advancing Computer Vision Applications>, which identifies interpretability and adversarial robustness as major barriers to real-world deployment in diverse domains including agriculture. Agricultural intelligence systems, therefore, cannot be developed in a vacuum; they must incorporate adversarial robustness as a core design principle, ensuring that models like those in fruit maturity estimation or yield prediction remain reliable when subjected to the dynamic and potentially noisy conditions of real-world field environments. The challenge remains to build systems that are not only accurate on clean data but also resilient to perturbations that could disrupt the critical "shift from experience-driven to digital farming."

#### Medical Image Analysis and Diagnosis

The escalating deployment of deep learning models within high-stakes medical environments has brought the dual imperatives of accuracy and trustworthiness to the forefront of computer vision research. While models such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have achieved superlative performance in detecting pathologies, their inherent "black-box" nature and susceptibility to perturbations pose significant barriers to clinical adoption. This subsection critically analyzes the evolution of architectures and methodologies designed to address these challenges, focusing on the transition from monolithic models to hybrid relational frameworks and the integration of rigorous defense mechanisms against adversarial threats. By synthesizing advancements in model interpretability and adversarial robustness, we highlight how modern systems ensure that diagnostic predictions are not only accurate but also transparent and secure.

To bolster both performance and the capacity for relational reasoning, researchers have increasingly turned to hybrid architectures that combine the strengths of disparate deep learning paradigms. A prime example of this innovative direction is the **MedicoCNN-GNN: A Hybrid Model with Integrated Attention Mechanism for Medical Image Classification**, which amalgamates CNNs with Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). In this framework, the CNN backbone serves to extract potent spatial features from medical images, while a Convolutional Spatial and Channel Block Attention (CSBAN) mechanism prioritizes critical dimensions. Concurrently, the GNN processes these features as a graph of tabular and relational data to capture inter-feature dependencies, achieving a test accuracy of 92%. This approach effectively operationalizes the insights from broader reviews of GNNs, such as **A review of graph neural networks: concepts, architectures, techniques, challenges, datasets, applications, and future directions**, which elucidate the message-passing mechanisms essential for understanding complex data structures. Yet, even a highly accurate and interpretable model can be rendered dangerous by adversarial attacks—imperceptible perturbations designed to mislead the network. The vulnerability of medical models is a specific instance of a broader problem detailed in surveys such as **Threat of Adversarial Attacks on Deep Learning in Computer Vision: A Survey** and **Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey**. In the specific domain of medical imaging, the integration of these robustness strategies is paramount. As noted in **Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey**, the "arms race" between attackers and defenders necessitates a shift toward strategies that are robust by design.

To construct truly trustworthy diagnostic systems, one must also consider the reliability of the model across different data distributions and input types. **Closely Relevant papers** such as **Deep learning-enabled medical computer vision** provide a broad overview of progress in medical imaging, establishing the baseline performance against which robustness is measured. #### Autonomous Systems and Scene Topology

The evolution of computer vision in autonomous systems marks a pivotal shift from simple object recognition to the complex task of holistic scene understanding. While traditional perception models have excelled at detecting and classifying discrete entities, they often lack the capacity to infer the relational context that defines dynamic environments, such as traffic scenarios. This limitation necessitates a deeper integration of relational reasoning, where the focus moves beyond individual objects to the topology of the scene—the structural and semantic relationships between elements. The transition reflects a broader trend in the field, where the limitations of isolated object detection are becoming increasingly apparent in safety-critical applications like autonomous driving.

In the realm of autonomous navigation, understanding "driving scene topology" requires reasoning about the connectivity of drivable paths and the assignment of traffic elements to those paths. <Graph-based Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes> introduces a pioneering framework, TopoNet, which formalizes this problem through a unified graph-based approach. Unlike prior methods that treated lane centerline detection and traffic sign classification as separate, disjoint tasks, TopoNet utilizes a Scene Graph Neural Network (SGNN) to model inter-instance relationships directly within the network. This approach moves beyond the pixel-level or bounding-box-level perception found in models described in <Deep learning for object detection and scene perception in self-driving cars: Survey, challenges, and open issues>, by instead learning a knowledge graph that encodes prior semantic and structural information. The integration of the Scene Knowledge Graph (SKG) allows the model to differentiate the influence of various road genome types—for instance, understanding that a "left turn" sign exerts a different influence on lane geometry than a "stop" sign. This method addresses a critical gap: the lack of a unified framework that can simultaneously perceive static geometry and infer dynamic navigational relationships. By leveraging Graph Neural Network (GNN) architectures, as surveyed in <A review of graph neural networks: concepts, architectures, techniques, challenges, datasets, applications, and future directions>, these systems can effectively perform message passing among nodes, allowing the "ego" lane to refine its estimated position based on the inferred location of a neighbor lane or a traffic light.

However, the reliance on GNNs for relational reasoning introduces a distinct set of vulnerabilities. While <Graph-based Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes> demonstrates that explicit relationship modeling (via SGNN) outperforms naive MLPs or post-processing-free methods, it also implicitly exposes the model to the adversarial threats that plague deep learning. The robustness of these relational models is a growing concern, as highlighted in <A Survey and Evaluation of Adversarial Attacks in Object Detection>. This survey highlights that detector robustness against white-box and black-box attacks are often uncorrelated, a finding that complicates the defense of systems dependent on accurate object perception to build their scene graphs. If the foundational object detection fails—compromised by adversarial noise—the resulting graph structure collapses. This "cascading vulnerability" is significant because it affects not just the perception of an object but the topological inference derived from it. For example, the previously mentioned survey notes that small objects are significantly more vulnerable than large ones (often suffering 10-20% greater mAP drops). In an autonomous driving context, where distant obstacles or subtle traffic signs are represented as small bounding boxes in the image plane, this vulnerability is particularly hazardous. The survey <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> further contextualizes this by noting that the high performance of deep models is paradoxically tied to their brittleness under perturbations. As models move from simple classification to complex graph reasoning, the attack surface expands from just the input image to the graph structures themselves.

To mitigate these risks, the research community has explored defenses that range from empirical hardening to certified guarantees. <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey> discusses the evolution of adversarial training and detection mechanisms. A notable concept is the "arms race" dynamic where defenses are eventually broken by stronger adaptive attacks. <A Survey and Evaluation of Adversarial Attacks in Object Detection> confirms this by showing that while ensemble methods improve attack transferability for attackers, they can also be used defensively. Using a diverse set of surrogate models significantly boosts transferability, suggesting that model diversity might be a double-edged sword. However, <Ensemble Methods as a Defense to Adversarial Perturbations Against Deep Neural Networks> argues that for defense, an ensemble increases robustness because an attack successful on one model does not necessarily fool others. Similarly, <Adversarial-Aware Deep Learning System Based on a Secondary Classical Machine Learning Verification Approach> proposes a hybrid verification system, using classical machine learning (which is immune to gradient-based attacks) to verify deep learning outputs, highlighting a shift toward hybrid architectures to ensure reliability.

The scope of adversarial threats extends beyond the digital domain into the physical world, where the "simulation-to-reality" gap is a major challenge. <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey> categorizes physical attacks into adversarial patches, optical attacks, and 3D-printed artifacts, introducing the conceptualization of an "adversarial medium." This is particularly relevant to autonomous systems interacting with the real world. Furthermore, the vulnerability is not limited to the visible spectrum. <Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems> demonstrates that models trained on infrared data are also susceptible to attacks like PGD, and that adversarial training remains a viable, albeit computationally intensive, defense. This expands the threat landscape, suggesting that hiding in different sensor modalities does not guarantee safety.

Interestingly, the push for robustness often aligns with the push for better feature extraction and architectural innovation. In medical imaging, a field as safety-critical as autonomous driving, <MedicoCNN-GNN: A Hybrid Model with Integrated Attention Mechanism for Medical Image Classification> shows that combining CNNs with GNNs enhances interpretability and accuracy. By using GNNs to capture inter-relationships among image features, and attention mechanisms (CSBAN) to prioritize critical spatial dimensions, the model becomes more robust against noise and improves classification trust. Similarly, in the agricultural domain, <Poisonous Mushroom Detection Using Graph Neural Networks> utilizes GNNs to discern toxic mushrooms by capturing nuanced pixel relationships, a task where precision is vital. These applications underscore the versatility of GNNs in processing high-dimensional data, as noted in <A survey of traditional and deep learning-based feature descriptors for high dimensional data in computer vision>, which highlights that higher dimensionality introduces structural complications solvable by advanced deep learning architectures.

Ultimately, the move toward autonomous systems and scene topology necessitates a convergence of relational reasoning, robust architectural design, and rigorous adversarial defense. We see a trajectory from pure perception to structured understanding, as exemplified by <Graph-based Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes>. This trajectory must be tempered by the security insights from works like <A Survey and Evaluation of Adversarial Attacks in Object Detection>, which reveal the specific vulnerabilities of detection pipelines (e.g., small object susceptibility). The path forward likely involves hybrid models, such as those combining classical verification with deep learning, or utilizing sensor fusion (like infrared and RGB) to cross-validate inputs. As models become "smarter" by understanding topology via SGNNs, they must also become more resilient, ensuring that the complex graph structures they reason with are not easily perturbed by imperceptible adversarial perturbations. The synthesis of these disparate research lines—relational graph learning, adversarial robustness, and physical-world validation—is essential for the safe deployment of autonomous systems.

#### Robot Vision and Safety-Critical Systems

The integration of deep learning into robotic vision systems represents a monumental leap in autonomy, yet it simultaneously exposes a critical class of safety vulnerabilities that transcend standard digital classification errors. Unlike static computer vision tasks, robot-vision operates within cyber-physical systems where perception errors can precipitate immediate physical consequences, ranging from operational failure to direct physical harm. Research such as <Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision? Adversarial Examples Against the iCub Humanoid> has empirically demonstrated that the very models enabling iCub to recognize objects are vulnerable to imperceptible adversarial perturbations. This vulnerability is not merely a software bug but a fundamental geometric instability in the learned feature mappings. To synthesize the broader landscape of adversarial robustness in computer vision, it is necessary to connect the specific findings on robotic platforms with the advanced methodologies of adversarial attack generation, the nuances of object detection, and the emerging theoretical frameworks that attempt to explain and mitigate these failures.

Deep learning models commonly employed in robotics, such as those based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), operate under the assumption that the underlying feature space is relatively smooth, meaning that small, imperceptible changes in the input image should result in correspondingly small changes in the internal representation. However, empirical evidence challenges this assumption. In the context of the iCub humanoid, <Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision? Adversarial Examples Against the iCub Humanoid> quantifies this phenomenon by contrasting the distances in the input space versus the deep feature space. Specifically, when an adversarial perturbation of a certain magnitude (e.g., L2 distance of 10) is applied to an image, the resulting change in the high-level feature representation (fc7 layer) is orders of magnitude larger than the change caused by a random noise vector of the same magnitude. This observation leads to the critical insight that the deep feature mapping is non-smooth and "violates the smoothness assumption." This geometric instability is the root cause of the vulnerability: the input images lie so close to decision boundaries that minimal, directional perturbations can push them deep into the manifold of a different class, appearing indistinguishable from legitimate high-confidence samples to the robotic observer.

The generation of these destabilizing inputs has evolved from simple gradient-based methods to sophisticated strategies that maximize confidence in misclassification. The algorithm proposed in <Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision? Adversarial Examples Against the iCub Humanoid> differs from traditional minimal-perturbation attacks by optimizing for maximum confidence under a perturbation budget. This is formalized by maximizing an objective function defined as $\Omega(x) = f_k(x) - \max_{l \neq k} f_l(x)$, where $k$ is the source class. By choosing to maximize the gap between the true class and the nearest competing class, the attack ensures the resulting adversarial example is not only misclassified but is pushed as far as possible into the decision region of the incorrect label. This approach is valuable for security auditing because it reveals the "worst-case" scenarios. It is distinct from methods that search for the smallest possible perturbation (which might be brittle), and instead provides a robust evaluation of where the model’s decision boundaries truly lie. Furthermore, this methodology allows for localized perturbations, which is a precursor to practical physical attacks, such as placing a sticker on an object that the robot must classify.

While the iCub study focuses on a hybrid system (deep features + SVM classifier), the fundamental vulnerability extends to end-to-end object detection architectures used in autonomous robotics and vehicles. The survey <A Survey and Evaluation of Adversarial Attacks in Object Detection> provides a comprehensive taxonomy of these threats, highlighting that object detectors are susceptible to both classification and localization failures. Unlike simple image classification, which outputs a single label, object detectors must predict bounding boxes and confidence scores, creating a larger attack surface. The non-smoothness of deep features affects localization similarly; a slight perturbation can cause a bounding box to disappear entirely or shift significantly, which is catastrophic for a mobile robot planning a collision-free path. The distinction between digital and physical attacks becomes blurred here; as detailed in <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World>, the perturbations must survive the rendering pipeline of a camera, accounting for noise, blurring, and lighting changes. Research in this area introduces the concept of "adversarial mediums"—the physical artifacts, such as printed patches or projected light—that carry the perturbation. The reliance on Expectation Over Transformations (EOT) to generate these robust perturbations underscores the difficulty of maintaining the geometric instability of the deep feature mapping across varying real-world viewpoints.

Analyzing the specific characteristics of these attacks reveals that the vulnerability is not uniform across the visual field. The "non-smoothness" of the deep feature mapping often manifests as high sensitivity in specific semantic regions. For instance, the "Sliding Window Gradient Sign Attack" demonstrates that adversarial perturbations are maximally effective when localized to key facial features like the eyes and nose. This suggests that the deep network’s reliance on specific, high-frequency features makes it brittle; perturbing these key tokens destabilizes the entire classification decision. This localized vulnerability is also observed in the context of adversarial patches, where a small, high-contrast patch applied to an object can override the global context, forcing the model to attend solely to the patch. The transferability of these attacks—where an adversarial example crafted on one model fools another—further complicates the security landscape. As noted in the survey on adversarial examples in computer vision, the survival of adversarial properties across different architectures suggests that these geometric flaws are shared among deep learners, likely due to the pursuit of similar optimization landscapes during training.

In safety-critical robotic systems, the ultimate goal is not just to classify or detect but to avoid disaster. Defense mechanisms must therefore prioritize the prevention of high-confidence errors over maximizing accuracy on benign inputs. The study on <Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision? Adversarial Examples Against the iCub Humanoid> proposes a practical defense mechanism known as "Classification with Reject Option." This method leverages the property of models like SVMs with RBF kernels, which act as Compact Abating Probability (CAP) models. The decision rule states that a sample is classified only if the maximum discriminant function value $f_{c*}(x)$ exceeds a threshold (typically zero). The intuition is that adversarial examples often inhabit "blind-spot" regions of the feature space—areas far from the training data yet confidently classified by the model. By setting a rejection threshold, the system can abstain from making a prediction when the input lies in low-density regions. Empirical results show that this defense does not merely degrade performance gracefully; surprisingly, it can sometimes *increase* accuracy for very small perturbations. Tiny perturbations are sufficient to push ambiguous samples out of the high-confidence region, triggering rejection and correctly identifying them as anomalous rather than misclassifying them.

However, the "Classification with Reject Option" is ultimately a patch on a fundamentally unstable geometry. It treats the symptom (low-confidence or anomalous inputs) rather than curing the disease (feature space non-smoothness). The broader literature on defenses reveals a similar dichotomy. Adversarial Training, widely considered the most robust empirical defense (as highlighted in <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks> and <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision>), explicitly trains the model on adversarial examples to smooth the decision boundary. Yet, this often comes at the cost of clean accuracy and computational expense. Other defenses, such as input purification or feature squeezing, attempt to remove the perturbation before it reaches the model. However, the survey <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision> notes the "arms race" dynamic: adaptive attacks can often bypass these defenses by accounting for the preprocessing steps. This reinforces the finding from the iCub analysis: unless the defense fundamentally alters the geometry of the deep feature mapping, it remains vulnerable to a determined attacker.

The implications of these vulnerabilities extend beyond static image processing to the dynamic and relational reasoning required in complex environments. Emerging architectures, such as Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), are increasingly used in robotics for scene understanding and topology reasoning. As detailed in <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision>, GNNs process relationships between objects, which is vital for autonomous navigation (e.g., understanding lane connectivity in <Graph-based Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes>). However, these models are not immune to the principles of geometric instability. Adversarial attacks on GNNs can manipulate the graph structure or node features to corrupt the relational reasoning. The hybridization of CNNs with GNNs, as seen in <MedicoCNN-GNN>, attempts to combine spatial feature extraction with relational learning. While this often improves performance, the defense mechanisms must address the vulnerability in both the spatial and relational domains.

Ultimately, the safety of robot vision systems hinges on a comprehensive defense strategy that acknowledges the inherent brittleness of current deep learning paradigms. The "Classification with Reject Option" offers an computationally efficient safeguard for hybrid systems, preventing the propagation of erroneous visual data into the robot's decision-making pipeline, thereby averting potential physical harm. This acts as a crucial fail-safe. However, for long-term robustness, the community must address the root cause identified by <Is Deep Learning Safe for Robot Vision? Adversarial Examples Against the iCub Humanoid>: the non-smoothness of the deep feature mapping. Future directions must therefore pivot towards "Geometric Adversarial Training," aiming to enforce Lipschitz continuity in the feature space. Only by stabilizing the geometry of how robots "see" the world can we transition from brittle systems that require constant rejection to robust agents capable of operating safely in the unpredictable physical world. As the field moves toward more complex multi-modal systems and transformers, maintaining this focus on the stability of the decision manifold, rather than just empirical accuracy, remains the paramount challenge for safety-critical AI.

#### Theoretical Gaps and Explainability

The absence of a unified theoretical framework for adversarial vulnerability constitutes the most significant obstacle to developing robust and trustworthy computer vision systems. While empirical research has produced a vast array of attacks and defenses, the literature remains fragmented regarding the fundamental causes of these phenomena. As noted in <Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review>, despite the proliferation of methods, there is a distinct lack of full theoretical understanding and interpretation of adversarial examples. This gap is frequently contrasted with the high performance of modern architectures, creating a dichotomy between empirical success and theoretical fragility. For instance, the recent review <A Short Review on Supervised Machine Learning and Deep Learning Techniques in Computer Vision> highlights that while deep learning offers superior feature automation compared to traditional supervised ML, it suffers from opacity and adversarial vulnerability, contrasting the "black box" nature of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with the interpretability of methods like SVMs or Random Forests. Consequently, the community has shifted focus from merely engineering new defenses to explaining the "why" behind these vulnerabilities, a transition that underscores the critical role of Explainable AI (XAI) in bridging the gap between empirical efficacy and user trust.

Current attempts to explain adversarial vulnerability coalesce around several competing geometric and statistical hypotheses. A primary line of reasoning, popularized by <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>, suggests that the high-dimensional geometry of neural networks is inherently linear, making it easy for an adversary to find small perturbations that cause large deviations along the decision boundary. This perspective is enriched by <A Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks>, which proposes that local neighborhoods on a DNN’s output surface have varying shapes depending on the input, necessitating metrics that quantify this geometric instability. Specifically, the observation that different inputs require different times to converge to adversarial samples has led to the development of metrics like the Adversarial Converging Time Score (ACTS), offering an attack-dependent evaluation of robustness. However, purely geometric interpretations are often complemented by the manifold hypothesis, explored in <A Survey of Adversarial Examples in Computer Vision: Attack, Defense, and Beyond>, which posits that adversarial examples lie on low-dimensional manifolds that are sparsely populated by training data, leaving large regions of the input space vulnerable. Furthermore, <The Limitations of Deep Learning in Adversarial Settings> formalizes the vulnerability by demonstrating that crafted samples can be generated with high success rates by precisely understanding the input-output mapping, challenging the assumption of smoothness in learned representations. A counter-hypothesis found in <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses> argues for the existence of "non-robust features"—features that are predictive for the correct class but are imperceptible to humans and easily corrupted. This suggests that DNNs learn statistical correlations rather than causal semantic structures, making them susceptible to perturbations that disrupt these brittle correlations.

The divergence between theoretical explanation and applied effectiveness becomes most apparent in the trade-off between adversarial robustness and standard generalization. The seminal work in <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks> established that adversarial training (optimizing for worst-case perturbations) invariably degrades performance on clean data, a finding echoed in the critical assessments of <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision>. This "robustness tax" implies that the features required for robust classification differ from those needed for high-accuracy classification on benign inputs. Consequently, researchers are investigating hybrid architectures to mitigate this. For example, <A Short Review on Supervised Machine Learning and Deep Learning Techniques in Computer Vision> advocates for hybrid approaches that leverage DL for feature extraction combined with interpretable ML classifiers to retain some level of trustworthiness. However, <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision> warns that many proposed defenses rely on "obfuscated gradients," which create a false sense of security. The authors of <Adversarial-Aware Deep Learning System Based on a Secondary Classical Machine Learning Verification Approach> propose a concrete hybrid solution, using a classical ML model (like Random Forest) as a secondary verifier, exploiting the hypothesis that classical models are immune to NN-targeted attacks. This aligns with the broader theoretical insight that stabilizing the decision manifold is essential for robust generalization, a premise supported by the non-robust feature analysis in <How Deep Learning Sees the World: A Survey on Adversarial Attacks & Defenses>. This dynamic highlights that theoretical gaps are not merely academic but have direct consequences on how we design and evaluate hybrid systems that aim to balance performance with interpretability and security.

As the limitations of purely empirical evaluations become clear, the field is increasingly valuing formal verification and certified robustness as theoretical anchors. Unlike empirical defenses (e.g., adversarial training), which can be broken by adaptive attacks, certified defenses provide mathematical guarantees that no perturbation within a specified norm bound can alter the prediction. The survey in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision> identifies this as a future direction, a stance reinforced by <Adversarial Perturbation Defense on Deep Neural Networks>, which reviews methods aiming to provide rigorous guarantees. The role of Explainable AI (XAI) is pivotal here; as noted in the context of <Interpreting Adversarial Examples in Deep Learning: A Review>, understanding the "why" is necessary to establish these guarantees. Visual explanation tools like Grad-CAM are increasingly dual-purposed not just for interpreting predictions but also for generating or detecting adversarial perturbations by highlighting local anomalies in gradient flow. This move toward explainability is distinct from the "black-box" attacks described in <Simple Black-Box Adversarial Perturbations for Deep Networks>, where the adversary treats the model as an oracle. Instead, modern theoretical frameworks treat the mechanism as transparent, analyzing the attack surface to certify robustness. The convergence of XAI and robustness is also evident in application-specific contexts; for example, <Mitigating Adversarial Attacks in Deepfake Detection> implies that understanding the perturbations that deceive a model is the first step in building reliable detection systems for high-stakes domains like media verification. Ultimately, the development of a unified theory requires synthesizing these geometric insights, formal guarantees, and explainability methods into a cohesive framework that moves beyond the "arms race" of adversarial examples.

#### Scalability, Efficiency, and Real-World Deployment

The transition from theoretical robustness to practical deployment of computer vision systems fundamentally alters the threat landscape, shifting focus from purely digital imperceptible perturbations to scenarios defined by computational constraints, physical realizability, and data privacy. As the field matures, the computational overhead of robust training protocols, such as adversarial training, becomes a prohibitive bottleneck for real-time or edge-based applications. Consequently, recent research has pivoted towards efficient inference and lightweight architectures that maintain a balance between performance and security. Furthermore, the necessity of aggregating diverse data for robust model training clashes with growing privacy regulations, positioning Federated Learning (FL) not just as a privacy-preserving utility but as a critical infrastructure for distributed adversarial robustness.

**Computational Challenges and the Efficiency Imperative**
Standard adversarial training methods, particularly those relying on Projected Gradient Descent (PGD), significantly increase training times and memory usage, often by an order of magnitude, compared to standard training. This inefficiency renders them infeasible for resource-constrained environments like mobile devices or autonomous drones. The literature acknowledges this trade-off, noting that while **Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks** established the efficacy of robust optimization, it did not address the computational scaling required for edge deployment. To bridge this gap, researchers are exploring two primary avenues: architectural efficiency and distillation. Lightweight architectures, such as MobileNet V2 utilized in **Classification of Skin Disease Using Deep Learning Neural Networks with MobileNet V2 and LSTM**, demonstrate that computational economy is possible, though securing these compact models remains an open challenge. Conversely, knowledge distillation was initially proposed as a defense mechanism in **Enhanced Attacks on Defensively Distilled Deep Neural Networks**, where the soft targets from a teacher model were intended to smooth decision boundaries. However, this study also revealed that distilled networks are not inherently robust; they merely alter the gradient landscape, which can be overcome by sophisticated attacks if not specifically hardened. Thus, the current trend involves developing "efficient robustness"—architectures that are inherently more resistant to perturbations without the massive overhead of iterative adversarial training, viewing robustness not just as a training strategy but as a structural property of the network.

**The Rise of Federated and Privacy-Preserving Learning**
The deployment of robust vision systems in healthcare and autonomous driving necessitates learning from distributed, sensitive data that cannot be centralized. **Optimizing Federated Graph Learning with Inherent Structural Knowledge and Dual-Densely Connected GNNs** highlights the severe challenges of Non-IID data distribution in Federated Learning (FL), particularly when dealing with complex graph-structured data. In FL, clients train models locally and share updates; however, adversarial manipulations can be injected at the local level, compromising the global model or disrupting convergence. The challenges are twofold: first, local defenses must be lightweight to avoid draining client battery or bandwidth; second, the aggregation server must distinguish between benign convergence drift and adversarial interference.

Furthermore, the review **A Survey of Adversarial Examples in Computer Vision: Attack, Defense, and Beyond** suggests that adversarial phenomena are not isolated to centralized datasets. In federated scenarios, transferability of attacks across heterogeneous models (which is typical in FL due to varying client hardware) becomes a critical vulnerability. Recent work proposes holding back specific training samples or gradients to act as a "canary" to detect malicious updates from compromised clients. However, as noted in **A Survey and Evaluation of Adversarial Attacks in Object Detection**, the heterogeneity of client data in safety-critical applications like autonomous driving makes standard aggregation dangerous. If one vehicle encounters a specific adversarial patch (e.g., a sticker on a stop sign), it must disseminate this knowledge without revealing the raw visual data. Privacy-preserving techniques, such as differential privacy, are being integrated into FL to mask gradients, yet this often conflicts with the need for precise gradient information required for effective adversarial detection. The tension necessitates frameworks that can perform robust aggregation while statistically guaranteeing that individual client data—the source of adversarial examples—cannot be reverse-engineered.

**Physical World Deployment and Environmental Robustness**
When moving from digital abstraction to physical deployment, the adversarial medium (the physical object or optical medium carrying the perturbation) dictates the attack strategy. **Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey** provides a comprehensive taxonomy of these challenges, categorizing attacks into Adversarial Patches, Optical attacks (Projected light/Lens filters), and 3D-printed objects. A key insight from this line of research is that digital robustness rarely translates to physical robustness due to the "Sim-to-Real" gap. The **Optimization for Robustness (EOT)** paradigm, which optimizes perturbations over a distribution of transformations (lighting, viewing angle), was an early attempt to solve this, but as noted in the subsequent critique within **Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey**, many physical defenses fail when evaluated against adaptive attacks tailored to the physical constraints.

For example, **Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems** demonstrates that vulnerabilities persist in non-standard spectra. Infrared systems used for surveillance or pedestrian detection are susceptible to perturbations that are invisible in the visible spectrum but manifest strongly in IR. This expands the threat model: physical deployment requires robustness across sensor modalities. Furthermore, the concept of the "adversarial medium" implies that the carrier of the attack must be physically realizable. **Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey** notes that while patches are highly effective and physically realizable, they are often conspicuous (visually ugly), limiting stealth. Conversely, optical attacks (projectors) offer an invisible vector but suffer from environmental constraints (sunlight washout). The consensus in recent surveys, such as **Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A survey**, is that physical attacks are evolving towards unrestricted adversarial examples—perturbations that are semantically meaningful (e.g., changing a natural texture) rather than noise—making detection significantly harder in real-world scenarios.

**Architectural Shifts: GNNs and Transformers in Robust Vision**
The evolution of architectures beyond standard CNNs introduces new robustness paradigms. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool for relational reasoning in vision, particularly in scene understanding and medical imaging. As synthesized in **On the Convergence of Graph Convolutional Networks: Trends, Limitations, and Future Directions**, GNNs process data as graphs of nodes and edges, which naturally suits object detection where spatial relationships are key. However, this structure introduces new attack vectors: adversarial attacks on GNNs can manipulate the graph structure itself (adding/removing nodes or edges) rather than just the node features or visual input.

For instance, **Graph-based Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes** utilizes GNNs to infer lane topology and traffic element relationships, moving beyond bounding boxes to understanding scene geometry. The robustness of these models is critical; if an adversarial patch on a car can also perturb the graph connectivity inferred by **TopoNet**, the vehicle might misinterpret the road topology entirely. Similarly, in medical imaging, **A Hybrid Dependable Deep Feature Extraction and Ensemble-Based Machine Learning Approach for Breast Cancer Detection** combines CNN features with graph-based reasoning to capture tissue inter-relationships. The vulnerability here is distinct: adversarial perturbations might not only misclassify a single image patch but could alter the inferred relational graph, misleading the diagnosis.

Moreover, the survey **A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision** highlights the emergence of Graph Transformers, which aim to combine the inductive bias of GNNs with the global attention mechanisms of Transformers. While Transformers like ViTs are powerful, they are computationally expensive. To address **Scalability, Efficiency, and Real-World Deployment**, researchers are proposing lightweight Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) and distinct architectures like **HL-HGAT** (Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network via Hodge-Laplacian Operator). The latter specifically addresses higher-order topological features (simplices like triangles), suggesting that robustness in future CV systems will depend on understanding these higher-order interactions, which are currently under-explored in adversarial robustness literature.

**Theoretical Insights and Evaluation Metrics**
Finally, the move towards deployment requires better evaluation metrics than standard success rate. **A Geometrical Approach to Evaluate the Adversarial Robustness of Deep Neural Networks** introduces **ACTS (Adversarial Converging Time Score)**, an attack-dependent metric that measures the "depth" of the robust region by timing how long it takes to converge to an adversarial sample. This is a crucial insight for real-time systems: a system might be robust (adversarial samples exist but are hard to find due to a flat loss landscape), which ACTS captures better than CLEVER.

Additionally, **Enhancing trustworthy deep learning for image classification against evasion attacks** argues for a shift from purely defensive strategies to understanding inherent vulnerabilities, proposing frameworks like **TPre-ADL** that leverage transferable pretrained adversarial features. This suggests a future direction where models are not merely hardened post-hoc but are "born robust" by design, utilizing pre-training on data that explicitly exposes these vulnerabilities.

In conclusion, the path to robust real-world deployment is paved with architectural innovation and privacy-centric learning. The synthesis of **Federated Graph Learning** strategies with **Lightweight Adversarial Training**, however, remains the most potent yet unsolved challenge. As indicated by the breadth of recent surveys, the field is moving beyond the "arms race" of attack/defense to a holistic view where efficiency, privacy, and physical realism are core design principles, not afterthoughts.

#### Conclusion: The Path to Trustworthy AI

The literature synthesized within this survey chronicles a dramatic escalation in the capabilities of adversarial actors, tracing a trajectory from rudimentary digital intrusions to sophisticated, multi-modal assaults that exploit the fundamental geometry of deep learning representations. Initially, the field was defined by the sensation of imperceptible perturbations crafted against digit classifiers, as demonstrated in seminal explorations of vulnerabilities in deep neural networks <Adversarial Examples: A Survey>. However, as detailed in <A Survey of Adversarial Examples in Computer Vision: Attack, Defense, and Beyond>, the attack surface has rapidly expanded. We have moved far beyond the simplicity of the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) to the targeted, iterative precision of Projected Gradient Descent (PGD), which remains the gold standard for evaluating robustness, as established in <Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks>. A particularly insidious evolution is the migration of these threats into the physical realm. As highlighted in <Physical Adversarial Attack Meets Computer Vision: A Decade Survey>, the introduction of the "adversarial medium"—be it a sticker, a projected light pattern, or a printed object—transforms abstract mathematical noise into tangible security risks. This shift forces the research community to confront the complexities of the physical world, where environmental factors like lighting and sensor characteristics introduce noise that attackers must overcome using techniques like Expectation Over Transformations (EOT). The survey findings in <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey> confirm that while physical attacks (e.g., <Visual Adversarial Attacks and Defenses in the Physical World: A Survey>) achieve high transferability, they often struggle with the stealth-naturalness trade-off, balancing the need for visual camouflage against the necessity of remaining visible enough to affect the sensor. Parallel to this, attackers have diversified into query-efficient black-box paradigms, where methods like <ZOO: Zeroth Order Optimization Based Black-box Attacks to Deep Neural Networks without Training Substitute Models> and <Simple Black-Box Adversarial Perturbations for Deep Networks> demonstrate that even restricted knowledge can cause failure, provided the attacker can treat the model as an oracle.

Compounding these external threats, the architectural evolution of computer vision models has simultaneously introduced novel vulnerabilities. The transition from pure Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to Transformer-based and Graph-based architectures has not eliminated the susceptibility to adversarial examples. In fact, the shift toward **Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)**, while offering superior relational reasoning capabilities as highlighted in <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision>, introduces new attack vectors. Adversarial examples in GNNs are not merely pixel perturbations; they often manifest as structural graph perturbations that manipulate node relationships. For instance,  utilizes Hodge-Laplacian operators to model higher-order simplices (edges, triangles), acknowledging that robust reasoning requires understanding multi-scale topology. However, this complexity makes such models vulnerable to targeted manipulation of the graph structure itself. Similarly, <Genetic-GSL: Genetic Graph Structure Learning> highlights the challenge of learning optimal graph topologies from noisy data; an adversary need not attack the node features directly but can poison the input graph structure to degrade performance. The susceptibility extends to emerging architectures; <Re-Think and Re-Design Graph Neural Networks in Spaces of Continuous Graph Diffusion Functionals> argues that the locality bias of standard GNNs limits their receptive fields, a weakness that attacks exploiting long-range dependencies can leverage. Consequently, the field is witnessing a convergence of adversarial robustness research with architectural search, where hybrid models like <MedicoCNN-GNN> attempt to fuse CNN features with GNN relational processing, yet must defend against attacks that target both the visual features and the semantic links between them.

Inside the security community, the realization that "obfuscated gradients" constitute a false sense of security has led to a pivot toward **Certified Defenses** and rigorous theoretical validation. <Advances in adversarial attacks and defenses in computer vision: A Survey> explicitly critiques the history of brittle defenses, noting that many were broken by adaptive attacks. The current path forward is paved with methods that provide mathematical guarantees: randomized smoothing and interval bound propagation (IBP) aim to certify that a region of the input space is free of perturbations. However, these approaches come with significant computational overheads and often poorer clean accuracy. To address the evaluation gap, metrics like  have been proposed to quantify robustness geometrically, moving beyond simple error rates to measure the time required to find an adversarial sample. Simultaneously, detection mechanisms are evolving. <Detecting Adversaries, yet Faltering to Noise?> explores using Conditional Variational AutoEncoders (CVAEs) to distinguish adversarial inputs from benign ones by analyzing the mismatch in latent distributions, a strategy that avoids the brittleness of input preprocessing. Furthermore, conceptual frameworks such as <Enhancing trustworthy deep learning for image classification against evasion attacks: a systematic literature review> advocate for shifting the focus from purely defensive patches to holistic trustworthiness, proposing models like TPre-ADL that inherently account for adversarial traits during the design phase rather than retrofitting defenses.

Finally, the necessity of adversarial robustness has become paramount in high-stakes, **domain-specific applications** where safety supersedes raw accuracy. In medical imaging, <MedicoCNN-GNN> illustrates that a misdiagnosis caused by an adversarial patch is a catastrophic failure, necessitating the integration of interpretability tools (like Grad-CAM) alongside robust training to ensure clinical viability. In autonomous driving, <Topology Reasoning for Driving Scenes> underscores that mere object detection is insufficient; understanding the relational topology of lanes and traffic elements via Scene Graph Neural Networks (SGNN) is crucial for safety. An adversarial attack that alters the relational graph could induce a vehicle to perceive a "stop" sign as disconnected from the lane, leading to disastrous consequences. Similarly, in agricultural monitoring, <FruitProm> demonstrates that perturbations can mask the continuous maturity states of crops, leading to yield loss. The vulnerability of these systems—even extending to infrared spectrums as noted in <Evaluating Adversarial Attacks and Defences in Infrared Deep Learning Monitoring Systems>—confirms that adversarial susceptibility is a fundamental property of high-dimensional learning manifolds, not just an artifact of RGB image processing. As we conclude, it is evident that achieving truly trustworthy AI requires a synthesis of these perspectives: leveraging the architectural expressiveness of GNNs and Transformers described in <A Survey on Graph Neural Networks and Graph Transformers in Computer Vision>, grounding them in the geometric insights of adversarial convergence, and validating them through the rigorous lens of certified robustness and physical-world simulation. Only by viewing adversarial defenses as a holistic engineering discipline—spanning from data manifolds to graph topologies and sensor physics—can we hope to deploy AI systems that are secure against the evolving threat landscape outlined in this survey.
