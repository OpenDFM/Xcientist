# LESS: Selecting Influential Data for Targeted Instruction Tuning

Mengzhou Xia  $^{1*}$  Sadhika Malladi  $^{1*}$  Suchin Gururangan  $^{2}$  Sanjeev Arora  $^{1}$  Danqi Chen

# Abstract

Instruction tuning has unlocked powerful capabilities in large language models (LLMs), using combined datasets to develop general-purpose chatbots. However, real-world applications often require a specialized suite of skills (e.g., reasoning). The challenge lies in identifying the most relevant data from these extensive datasets to effectively develop specific capabilities, a setting we frame as targeted instruction tuning. We propose LESS, an optimizer-aware and practically efficient algorithm to estimate data influences and perform Low-rank gradEnt Similarity Search for instruction data selection. Crucially, LESS adapts existing influence formulations to work with the Adam optimizer and variable-length instruction data. LESS first constructs a highly reusable and transferable gradient datastore with low-dimensional gradient features and then selects examples based on their similarity to few-shot examples embodying a specific capability. Experiments show that training on a LESS-selected  $5\%$  of the data can often outperform training on the full dataset across diverse downstream tasks. Furthermore, the selected data is highly transferable: smaller models can be leveraged to select useful data for larger models and models from different families. Our qualitative analysis shows that our method goes beyond surface form cues to identify data that exemplifies the necessary reasoning skills for the intended downstream application. To facilitate future work, we release code and data at princeton.nlp/LESS.

# 1. Introduction

Instruction tuning has made large language models (LLMs) adept at following human instructions (Ouyang et al., 2022) as versatile chatbots (OpenAI, 2022; 2023; Anthropic, 2023; Google, 2023). Recent efforts curating highly diverse and wide-ranging instruction tuning datasets (Taori et al., 2023; Wang et al.; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023, inter alia) induce remarkably strong generalization even from a small number of examples (Zhou et al., 2023). Regardless, it remains an open problem to understand how to best utilize these various datasets.

Many real-world applications call for cultivating a specific suite of capabilities in LLMs (e.g., reasoning skills). However, training LLMs with mixed instruction tuning datasets can hinder the development of these specific capabilities. For example, Wang et al. (2023b) demonstrates that LLMs trained on a mix of instruction tuning datasets exhibit worse performance than those trained on a subset of the data. Additionally, considering the broad spectrum of user queries and the multitude of skills required to respond to them, there may not always be enough in-domain data available. Therefore, we hope to be able to effectively use the general instruction tuning data to improve specific capabilities. We frame this setting as targeted instruction tuning:

Given just a handful of examples embodying a specific capability, how can we effectively select relevant fine-tuning data from a large collection of instruction datasets?

We approach this problem by prioritizing training on data that directly minimizes loss on a target task instead of relying on surface form features (Gururangan et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2023b). Inspired by past works estimating the influence of individual training datapoints with gradient information (Pruthi et al., 2020; Han et al., 2023), we design an optimizer-aware approach to select such data. However, straightforward application of this influence formulation faces several challenges unique to the instruction tuning setting: (1) LLMs are traditionally fine-tuned with the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) instead of the canonical SGD optimizer; (2) using sequence-level gradients of variable-length instruction data can derail the influence estimation; and (3) the large number of trainable parameters in LLMs makes the computation and storage of gradient information extremely resource-intensive.

We address these concerns in LESS, an algorithm that performs Low-rank gradiEnt Similarity Search to select relevant instruction tuning data for a target application, which exhibits the following properties:

1. Compatible with instruction tuning with Adam (\$2 and §3): LESS adapts the gradient features from classical influence formulations (Pruthi et al., 2020) to work with the Adam optimizer and variable-length instruction data. The optimization insights and influence formulation may be of independent interest as well.  
2. Efficient (\$4.1): LESS uses LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and random projections (Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984) to construct a gradient dataset with low-dimensional, easily manipulable gradient features that permit efficient and effective dataset selection. The gradient dataset can be reused for new target tasks.  
3. Transferable (\$5.3): Data selected using small models' gradient features induce strong performance in large models and models from different families, adding to the efficiency of LESS (Table 2).  
4. Interpretable ( $\S 6.2$ ): Qualitative analysis shows that LESS selects data with similar reasoning and skill types as the target task, whereas existing approaches often select data based on surface form cues (e.g., language or topic).

We evaluate our approach on three diverse downstream datasets—MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), TYDIQA (Clark et al., 2020), and BBH (Suzgun et al., 2023)—each containing distinct subtasks that effectively simulate targeted instruction tuning scenarios. Results show that LESS often selects a small subset of the data (5%) that outperforms training on the full dataset, and the selected subset remains universally effective across model scales and families (Table 2). Comparisons with other data selection methods show that LESS is the only consistently effective approach, justifying its relatively high computational cost.

# 2. Preliminaries: Influence Formulation

We restate the approach in Pruthi et al. (2020) of using a first-order approximation of training dynamics to estimate the influence of a training datapoint on held-out data.<sup>1</sup>

Per-step influence. Consider a model  $\pmb{\theta}^t$  at time step  $t$  trained on the loss  $\ell (\cdot ;\pmb{\theta}^t)$ . We can write the first-order Taylor expansion of the loss on a validation datapoint  $\pmb{z}^{\prime}$  as

$$
\ell (\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t + 1}) \approx \ell (\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}) + \langle \nabla \ell (\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}), \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t + 1} - \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t} \rangle
$$

For ease of exposition, assume that we are training the model with SGD with batch size 1 and learning rate  $\eta_t$ . If  $z$  is the training data at time step  $t$ , we can write the SGD update as  $\pmb{\theta}^{t+1} - \pmb{\theta}^t = -\eta_t \nabla \ell(z; \pmb{\theta}^t)$ . Then, the Taylor expansion can be written as

$$
\ell (\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t + 1}) - \ell (\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}) \approx - \eta_ {t} \langle \nabla \ell (\boldsymbol {z}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}), \nabla \ell (\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}) \rangle
$$

Trajectory influence. The influence of  $z$  over the entire training run can be measured by aggregating the influence at every training step that uses  $z$ . Since  $z$  is used once per epoch, it is natural to express this as a summation over epochs:

$$
\operatorname {I n f} _ {\mathrm {S G D}} \left(\boldsymbol {z}, \boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}\right) \triangleq \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {N} \bar {\eta} _ {i} \left\langle \nabla \ell \left(\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}\right), \nabla \ell \left(\boldsymbol {z}; \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}\right) \right\rangle \tag {1}
$$

where  $\bar{\eta}_i$  is the learning rate used during the  $i$ th epoch out of  $N$  total training epochs and  $\theta_i$  is the model after the  $i$ th epoch of training.

Data selection with influence. While Pruthi et al. (2020) used this insight to identify unlabeled training data, we instead apply this formula to design a data selection strategy. In particular, at each time step  $t$ , selecting  $z$  to maximize  $\langle \nabla \ell(z'; \theta^t), \nabla \ell(z; \theta^t) \rangle$  will drive a larger decrease in the loss on the validation point  $z'$ . However, when computing  $\operatorname{Inf}_{\mathrm{SGD}}$  across several epochs, we note that the model checkpoints  $\{\theta_i\}$  after the first epoch will depend on the dataset selected for training. This causes the data selection problem to become circular, and we empirically circumvent this problem with a short warmup training run on a randomly selected  $\mathcal{D}_{\text{warmup}} \subset \mathcal{D}$  for  $N = 4$  epochs (see §4.1). Overall, this data selection strategy is especially useful in the transfer learning setting, because it does not require any specific relationship between  $z'$  and  $z$ . The next two sections describe how we adapt this basic approach to operate efficiently and effectively with instruction tuning.

# 3. LESS: Estimating the Influence of Instructions

Here, we describe how LESS adapts the influence formulation in Equation (1) to select instructions that effectively induce a target capability. We consider two main conceptual challenges here: (1) past works described in Section 2 focus on the SGD optimizer, but LLMs are traditionally tuned using Adam (§3.1), and (2) instruction tuning exhibits abnormal gradients that cause the prior formulation to heavily favor selecting shorter instructions, thereby hurting performance (§3.2).

Notation. We follow the standard autoregressive setting for instruction tuning, where a datapoint  $z$  consists of an instruction  $s$  and a completion  $c$ . For each data point, the loss  $\ell(z; \theta)$  is the cross entropy between the model distribution  $p(\cdot | s)$  and the true label  $c$ . When  $c$  contains multiple tokens, we use an average of the token-wise cross-entropies in the completion as the loss of  $z$ . We use  $\theta^t$  to indicate the model at the  $t$ -th step, and we use  $\theta_i$  to indicate the model after the  $i$ th epoch of training.

# 3.1. Extension to Adam

The formulation in Equation (1) is unique to optimizing models with SGD. However, instruction tuning is usually performed using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015). In this case, the parameter update at a given step is:

$$
\boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t + 1} - \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t} = - \eta_ {t} \Gamma (\boldsymbol {z}, \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t})
$$

$$
\Gamma (\boldsymbol {z}, \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}) \triangleq \frac {\boldsymbol {m} ^ {t + 1}}{\sqrt {\boldsymbol {v} ^ {t + 1} + \epsilon}}
$$

$$
\boldsymbol {m} ^ {t + 1} = \left(\beta_ {1} \boldsymbol {m} ^ {t} + \left(1 - \beta_ {1}\right) \nabla \ell (\boldsymbol {z}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t})\right) / \left(1 - \beta_ {1} ^ {t}\right)
$$

$$
\pmb {v} ^ {t + 1} = (\beta_ {2} \pmb {v} ^ {t} + (1 - \beta_ {2}) \nabla \ell (\pmb {z}; \pmb {\theta} ^ {t}) ^ {2}) / (1 - \beta_ {2} ^ {t})
$$

where all operations are performed elementwise, with  $\beta_{1}$  and  $\beta_{2}$  as the hyperparameters for the first and second moments, respectively, and  $\epsilon$  as a small constant. Then, the first-order expansion for the Adam dynamics suggests we should choose  $z$  to maximize  $\langle \nabla \ell (z';\pmb{\theta}^t),\Gamma (z,\pmb{\theta}^t)\rangle$ . Note that extending the data selection strategy to Adam exacerbates the aforementioned circularity of the procedure, because computing  $\Gamma (z,\pmb {\theta})$  requires accessing the  $m$  and  $v$  terms, which are determined by prior training gradients. As before, we obtain these from the warmup training (§4.1).<sup>5</sup>

# 3.2. Addressing Abnormalities in Sequence-Level Gradients

A faithful application of Section 2 to the instruction tuning setting would consider that the gradient of an example  $z$  is the average of the gradients of each token in the completion. In particular, we would choose which tokens to train on within each sequence in order to best reduce the validation loss, and notably, optimizing the inner product at a fine-grained level may lead to better results. However, estimating individual token influences is extremely expensive because it requires computing the gradient of a vector of token losses with respect to the model parameters, so we instead perform data selection at the sequence level.

Because the example gradient is the average of many token gradients, the gradient norm of the sequence  $\| \nabla \ell (\boldsymbol {z};\boldsymbol{\theta}^t)\|$  is negatively correlated with the length of the completion (Figures 3 and 4). This is a well-known issue for influence formulations that compute averaged token gradients for sequences of varying lengths. We discuss this observation further in Appendix H but defer an in-depth study to future work. This phenomenon causes the influence in Equation (1) to heavily upweight shorter training sequences, which in turn leads to markedly worse performance (see Table 13). To mitigate this problem, we normalize the gradient features in LESS and use the cosine similarity instead of the dot product to estimate influences.

Definition 3.1 (Adam Influence). Suppose the model is trained for  $N$  epochs, where  $\bar{\eta}_i$  is the average learning rate in the  $i$ th epoch and  $\theta_i$  is the model checkpoint after the  $i$ th epoch. We define the influence of a training datapoint  $z$  on a validation datapoint  $z'$  when training with Adam as

$$
\operatorname {I n f} _ {\operatorname {A d a m}} \left(\boldsymbol {z}, \boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}\right) \triangleq \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {N} \bar {\eta} _ {i} \cos \left(\nabla \ell \left(\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}\right), \Gamma (\boldsymbol {z}, \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i})\right)
$$

where  $\cos$  computes the cosine similarity of the two vectors.

# 4. LESS: Estimating Influences Efficiently

We have established how to estimate the influence using model gradients, but given the sheer size of LLMs, computing and storing model gradients is still prohibitively expensive. In this section, we describe the design choices that make LESS computationally efficient, and illustrate the computing steps in Figure 1.

We use LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to efficiently perform the warmup training. Then, we construct a gradient dataset of projected low-dimensional gradient features (Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984; Park et al., 2023) that can be reused for different target tasks. Finally, we detail how our data selection algorithm uses the dataset to construct a training dataset that accommodates substructures (e.g., individual reasoning skills) in the target validation set.

Setting. Our goal is to select a subset  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{train}}$  of a large, diverse instruction tuning dataset  $\mathcal{D}$  such that training a model on  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{train}}$  achieves lower loss on a target validation dataset  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}$ , and therefore performs well on the test data.  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}$  can contain several subtasks  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}^{(1)}, \ldots, \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}^{(m)}$  (e.g., question answering for multiple languages). The validation set is fixed and only contains a few examples per subtask (e.g., 1 example per language in TYDIQA). We use a held-out test set  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}$  to evaluate the performance of the model trained

# 4.1. Efficient Gradient Feature Computation

Computing Definition 3.1 requires taking the inner product of two vectors with a size of trainable parameters in the model. Directly using such high-dimensional gradient vectors as features for dataset selection is very computationally expensive, so we apply two techniques to construct meaningful low-dimensional gradient features: parameter efficient fine-tuning via LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and random projections (Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984; Park et al., 2023). We apply these techniques to  $\nabla \ell(z'; \theta)$  for validation datapoints  $z'$  and to  $\Gamma(z, \theta)$  for training datapoints  $z$ . To enable rapid transfer to new target validation tasks, we create a datastore with these low-dimensional features for candidate training datapoints.

Step 1: Warmup training with LoRA. We use LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to reduce the number of trainable parameters and accelerate the inner products in Definition 3.1. LoRA freezes the pre-trained weights and adds a low-rank adaptor to linear layers throughout the network. We use LoRA to instruction tune a pre-trained base model (e.g., LLAMA-2-7B) on a random subset  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{warmup}} \subset \mathcal{D}$  for  $N$  epochs (we only use  $5\%$  of the training data in practice, see §5.1), checkpointing the model after each epoch to store  $\{\pmb{\theta}_i\}_{i=1}^N$ . The gradient when training with LoRA, denoted  $\hat{\nabla}\ell(\cdot; \pmb{\theta}) \in \mathbb{R}^P$ , is much lower dimensional than the model itself; for example, in LLAMA-2-7B,  $\hat{\nabla}\ell(\cdot; \pmb{\theta})$  is less than  $2\%$  the size of  $\pmb{\theta}$ . We use  $\hat{\nabla}\ell(\cdot; \pmb{\theta})$  to compute the Adam update and denote it as  $\hat{\Gamma}(\cdot, \pmb{\theta})$ . This initial warmup training is motivated conceptually in §3.1, and empirical results in §6.1 demonstrate that omitting it yields suboptimal results.

Step 2: Projecting the gradients. To further reduce the feature dimensionality, we apply a random projection to the LoRA gradients. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss Lemma (Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984) asserts that such projections often preserve the inner products in Definition 3.1, thereby ensuring these low-dimensional gradient features are still useful for dataset selection. For a given validation datapoint  $z'$  and model checkpoint  $\theta_i$ , we can compute a  $d$ -dimensional projection of the LoRA gradient  $\tilde{\nabla}\ell(z';\theta_i) = \Pi^\top \hat{\nabla}\ell(z';\theta_i)$ , with each entry of  $\Pi \in \mathbb{R}^{P\times d}$  drawn from a Rademacher distribution (i.e.,  $\Pi_{ij} \sim \mathcal{U}(\{-1,1\})$ ). For training datapoints  $z$ , we compute  $\tilde{\Gamma}(z,\cdot) = \Pi^\top \hat{\Gamma}(z,\cdot)$ .

We use the memory-efficient online implementation of random projections from Park et al. (2023) to compute and apply  $\Pi$ . In practice, we choose  $d = 8192$ .

# 4.2. Data Selection Algorithm

The data selection strategy (see step 3 in Figure 1) adapts the approach in Section 2 to efficiently operate on subtasks of the validation set (e.g., individual tasks within BBH). For each subtask  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}^{(j)}$ , we compute its average gradient feature for every model checkpoint  $\theta_{1}, \ldots, \theta_{N}$ :

$$
\bar {\nabla} \ell (\mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm {v a l}} ^ {(j)}; \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}) = \frac {1}{| \mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm {v a l}} ^ {(j)} |} \sum_ {\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime} \in \mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm {v a l}} ^ {(j)}} \tilde {\nabla} \ell (\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}).
$$

As in Definition 3.1, we then aggregate the scores of how close the given datapoint is to each validation subtask throughout training. For example, we can adapt Definition 3.1 to write

$$
\operatorname {I n f} _ {\mathrm {A d a m}} (\boldsymbol {z}, \mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm {v a l}} ^ {(j)}) = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {N} \bar {\eta} _ {i} \frac {\left\langle \bar {\nabla} \ell \left(\mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm {v a l}} ^ {(j)} ; \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}\right) , \tilde {\Gamma} (\boldsymbol {z} , \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}) \right\rangle}{\| \bar {\nabla} \ell \left(\mathcal {D} _ {\mathrm {v a l}} ^ {(j)} ; \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}\right) \| \| \tilde {\Gamma} (\boldsymbol {z} , \boldsymbol {\theta} _ {i}) \|}. \tag {2}
$$

We select training datapoints that can improve performance on any one of the validation subtasks. Following the logic in §2, we compute the score for  $\mathbf{z}$  as the maximum across all subtasks:  $\max_j \operatorname{Inf}_{\mathrm{Adam}}(\mathbf{z}, \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}^{(j)})$ . We select the highest

scoring examples to construct  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{train}}$ . After selection, we use the selected subset  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{train}}$  to train the target model  $\mathcal{M}_T$ .

# 5.2. Baselines

We compare LESS with a several baselines (see more details in Appendix C). The simplest baseline is random selection, where we randomly sample data from the training set for instruction tuning. We also compare to BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009), which featurizes examples by their word frequency statistics (i.e., TF-IDF) to rank the training instances, and select the top  $k$  data points with the highest scores to construct  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{train}}$ . Another baseline is DSIR (Xie et al., 2023b), which uses  $n$ -gram features to weight candidate training data  $\mathcal{D}$ . We resample  $k$  data points according to the importance weights. We also compare to RDS (Representation-based Data Selection) (Zhang et al., 2018; Hanawa et al., 2020), which uses model's hidden representations as features for data selection. For fair comparison, we compute the similarity score using Equation (2) but replace the gradient features with the final layer representations of the last token of each sequence. These features are 2048-dimensional in LLAMA-2-7B. Another intuitive baseline we consider is to directly use the validation data  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}$  for training, but this drastically degrades performance (Appendix D.3).

Data selected using a small model boosts performance for larger and different models. Table 2 evaluates several LESS-T settings. We use LLAMA-2-7B as the selection model  $\mathcal{M}_S$  and train either LLAMA-2-13B (i.e., a larger model) and MISTRAL-7B (i.e., a different model family) as the target model  $\mathcal{M}_T$ . LESS-T outperforms random selection and has a relatively small gap compared to LESS (i.e., directly using LLAMA-2-13B or MISTRAL-7B as the selection model  $\mathcal{M}_S$ ). The strong performance of LESS-T indicates that LESS selects generally useful data, so data selection costs can be further amortized across different models. Conceptually, our derivation in Section 2 states that the dataset selected using one model induces strong performance in another one when the inner products of the gradients of different examples are roughly equal for  $\mathcal{M}_S$  and  $\mathcal{M}_T$ . Our experiments support that this finding is true, thereby adding to the growing body of work that small models can effectively select data for other models during pre-training (Xie et al., 2023a; Engstrom et al., 2024) and in-context learning (Wang et al., 2023a).

LESS is the only consistently effective approach compared to others. In Table 3, we compare LESS to several baselines. We observe that conventional approaches relying on word frequency statistics (e.g., BM25),  $n$ -gram features (e.g., DSIR), or representations (e.g., RDS) show minimal improvement over random selection, and LESS consistently outperforms the strongest baseline. We acknowledge that the baseline methods are less computationally expensive. Nonetheless, our findings suggest that instruction data calls for a refined definition of similarity between data points (i.e., the influence formulation in Definition 3.1). Although the baseline approaches typically select data that is topically or semantically similar to the query and completion, qualitative analysis (\$6.2) indicates that LESS is better at selecting data that require the same kind of reasoning process (i.e., how the instruction is executed) as the target task  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}$ .

Please find more details in Appendix D.6.

# 6.1. Computational Complexity

Table 4 describes the asymptotic complexity, wall-clock runtime, and the storage cost required for each step of LESS. The reported wall-clock time is measured in single A100 (80GB) GPU hours. The gradient feature computation is the most expensive step, and the cost scales linearly with candidate dataset size  $|\mathcal{D}|$ , number of checkpoints  $N$ , and gradient dimension  $d$ . Storing the resulting gradient data-tore consumes memory that scales linearly with  $d$ . Warmup training is also relatively expensive, and the complexity scales with  $|\mathcal{D}_{\text{warmup}}|$  and  $N$ . However, the expenses incurred during these two stages are one-time costs that can be amortized over many target tasks.

In the following sections, we explore how our choices for  $|\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{warmup}}|$ ,  $N$ , and  $d$  impact the performance of LESS. In summary, increasing  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{warmup}}$  and  $N$  improves the accuracy of the influence estimation (Definition 3.1), but doing so also increases the cost of the warmup training step. Similarly, increasing  $d$  improves the utility of the projected gradient features (Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984), but doing so also scales the memory and compute required for LESS.

Warmup training is crucial. The warmup training step tunes a model on  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{warmup}}$ , which is usually a random  $5\%$  of the data  $\mathcal{D}$ . We investigate: (1) if this step is necessary, and (2) if using more data further enhances performance.

# 6.2. Qualitative Analysis

We manually compare the data selected by LESS and baseline approaches. Table 16 shows top examples selected by BM25, RDS, and LESS, given a single Bengali question-answering example from the TYDIQA dataset. BM25 and RDS simply match the few-shot data  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}$  and select Bengali examples from different tasks (mask recovery and hate speech detection, respectively). In contrast, LESS selects an English example from an open-book question-answering task similar to TYDIQA. This example demonstrates that LESS selects examples with a similar underlying reasoning type without relying heavily on surface textual or linguistic cues. Such examples enable effective zero-shot transfer to the Bengali language, despite being in a different language (Hu et al., 2020). Furthermore, we observe that LESS chooses more examples from the DOLLY dataset for TYDIQA, likely because DOLLY contains many open-book question-answering examples. Our findings suggest that LESS may be used to improve interpretability of large, general-purpose instruction tuning datasets. Appendix K contains more analysis details.

# 7. Related Work

Curating high-quality instruction tuning data. Using high-quality instruction tuning data can dramatically improve base LLMs. Instruction tuning data is generally of two types: (1) task-based datasets curated from traditional NLP tasks (Wang et al., 2022; Sanh et al., 2022; Wei et al.,

2022b; Longpre et al., 2023), and (2) open-ended instruction following datasets, broadly covering a wide range of topics (Taori et al., 2023; Conover et al., 2023; Kopf et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023). Increasing data quality and diversity instead of quantity has been shown to more effectively induce instruction following abilities (Cao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Bukharin & Zhao, 2023; Du et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). Moreover, (Han, 2023; Lin et al., 2023a) have demonstrated that in-context learning alone can significantly advance instruction following. Our work adds to this narrative by selecting high-quality, relevant data from a large pool of available datasets to induce a particular capability in the model.

Coresets and gradient-based data selection. Data selection has been viewed as a coreset selection problem (Phillips, 2017), which aims to find a subset of training examples that induces performance similar to training on the full dataset (Toneva et al., 2018; Sener & Savarese, 2018; Coleman et al., 2019; Kaushal et al., 2019; Xia et al., 2020; Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Killamsetty et al., 2021a,b; Paul et al., 2021; Meding et al., 2021; Mindermann et al., 2022; Sorscher et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2023). These works focus on in-domain coreset selection, whereas our work involves transfer learning. Several prior works use pre-defined notions of useful data (Gururan-gan et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023b) or n-gram features (Xie et al., 2023b) to select pre-training examples, but LESS more closely resembles Mirzasoleiman et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2020); Yu et al. (2020b); Killamsetty et al. (2021a) in its reliance on gradient information. Our work is closest to Han et al. (2023), which used model gradients to select pretraining data to improve in-context learning performance. However, our offline approach enables extremely efficient adaptation to new downstream tasks.

Data attribution and influence functions. Our formulation of influence has been used in identifying mislabeled examples (Pruthi et al., 2020), analyzing memorization effects (Feldman & Zhang, 2020), and deriving various interpretability insights (Madsen et al., 2022), but it does not permit counterfactual reasoning. On the other hand, influence functions (Hampel, 1974) can counterfactually reason about model behaviors and training data (Koh & Liang, 2017), but some studies have found limitations (Bae et al., 2022) in its robustness (Basu et al., 2020) and effectiveness (Ilyas et al., 2022). In the LLM setting, influence functions are computationally expensive (Grosse et al., 2023), though concurrent work in Engstrom et al. (2024) proposed using an efficient influence function estimation (Park et al., 2023) to select pre-training data (see Appendix G for a detailed comparison of influence functions and our influence formulation). Our findings suggest that first-order influence

approximations (Pruthi et al., 2020) are effective for data selection in transfer learning settings (Han et al., 2023).

# 8. Discussion and Future Work

We propose a data selection algorithm LESS based on optimizer-aware influences (§2 and §3). LESS creates a datastore of effective and reusable low-dimensional gradient features to enable efficient data selection (§4). Experiments in §5 demonstrate the effectiveness of LESS compared to relevant baselines and also highlight the ability to use smaller models to select data to train larger models (Table 2). Analysis and ablations in §6 demonstrate that our method selects more interpretable data but can be computationally expensive. We discuss the limitations in detail in Section 8.

In the future, LESS could be effectively used for test-time adaptation, allowing the model to adapt on data selected based on the context and demonstrations provided by the users. Additionally, we could modify the data selection procedure to minimize any differentiable metric (e.g., a surrogate for toxicity and harmfulness) instead of just cross-entropy loss. Moreover, our experiments and concurrent findings in Engstrom et al. (2024) may prompt further investigation of the utility of gradient features as opposed to surface-form cues in data selection. Insights into the optimization characteristics and sequence-level gradient phenomena (§3.2) are likely of theoretical interest.

Our observation that tuning on more data can degrade performance on a particular task may also be intimately connected to the catastrophic forgetting behaviors observed in LLMs (Lin et al., 2023b).

# Limitations

We discuss the limitations of our approach here.

1. Warmup training phase is required. The warmup training phase is crucial for obtaining useful gradient features for data selection. It is evident that we must fine-tune the base model to align with the specific data distribution we intend to select from, because directly utilizing gradients from pre-trained models like Llama-2-7B or Llama-2-7B-CHAT is not as effective (Table 5). This additional step introduces increased complexity and computational load to LESS.

2. Average gradients over completion tokens. The average gradient of all the completion tokens is used as a surrogate to represent the entire sequence. In scenarios involving longer sequences in training or validation data, such as open-ended generation, this aggregation could become more ambiguous and less effective. In Section 3.2, we highlight how this operation can cause anomalies in the data selection pipeline.

3. Minimizing loss does not monotonically increase performance. Unlike standard vision settings, minimizing the validation loss (i.e., cross entropy loss) does not always improve the task performance in instruction tuning. This is because instruction tuning tasks can generally require the model to produce long-form generations. Although perplexity aligns well with task performance in base LLMs (Xia et al.), but the relationship is much more obscure in fine-tuning tuning or instruction tuning (Tay et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023). LESS is motivated by selecting datapoints that minimize the validation loss (Section 2) and this strategy often induces models with high accuracy (Table 2), but minimizing loss does not monotonically improve accuracy (see our discussion in Appendix I).

4. Linearity in data selection. The first-order approximation in Section 2 ignores the effect of adding multiple datapoints together. In particular, two duplicate points would be scored equally highly and thought to doubly improve the model, though this is likely not the case. Preliminary theory in Saunshi et al. (2023) discusses when influences may add linearly. However, several more sophisticated algorithms have been proposed to more flexibly model interactions across datapoints at a significantly higher computational cost (Søgaard et al., 2021; Guu et al., 2023). Unfortunately, such algorithms are too expensive in the language model setting.

# A.2. Training Details

All experiments were conducted using the parameter-efficient fine-tuning method LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). We employed a learning rate scheduler with linear warm-up and cosine decay, reaching a peak learning rate of  $2 \times 10^{-5}$ . A batch size of 128 was used, and training was carried out for 4 epochs across all selected datasets. We initially conducted experiments to align the number of training steps between small and large datasets. However, increasing the number of epochs on small datasets did not yield performance improvements. Consequently, we opted for a fixed number of epochs in all experiments.

For the LoRA module, we specified a rank of 128, an  $\alpha$  value of 512, a dropout rate of 0.1, and learned LoRA matrices for all attention matrices. This configuration resulted in 135 million trainable parameters for LLAMA-2-7B, constituting  $1.95\%$  of the original LLAMA-2-7B model. For MISTRAL-7B, there were 109 million trainable parameters, accounting for  $1.48\%$  of the original MISTRAL-7B model. Lastly, the LLAMA-2-13B model comprised 209 million trainable parameters, representing  $1.59\%$  of the original LLAMA-2-13B model.

In each experiment, we performed three trials using distinct random seeds. When employing random selection approaches, this entailed selecting three different random subsets from the training dataset. In the case of our approach (LESS), it involved conducting warmup training with various subsets of the training data and subsequently selecting different subsets for each trial from each warmup-trained model. We used consistent optimization seeds across all experiments.

# B. Evaluation Details

We follow Wang et al. (2023b) to evaluate the performance of the models on the target tasks. For MMLU, we measure the 5-shot accuracy of the test set averaged across 57 subtasks. For TYDIQA, we measure the 1-shot macro-averaged F1 score across all 11 languages. We adopt the gold-passage setup where one passage containing the reference answer is provided to the model. For BBH, we report the average 3-shot exact match score across all tasks. Chain-of-thought reasoning is provided in each in-context learning example to prompt the model to generate chain-of-thought reasoning traces for test examples. We evaluate on the validation set  $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{val}}$  (the same reference set used for data selection) at the end of each epoch and select the best checkpoint to evaluate on the final test set for each experiment. Note that this procedure might introduce some bias to the final test set, given that the validation set is relatively small (e.g., TYDIQA only has 9 validation examples in total). However, in practice, we don't find this bias to affect the comparisons between different methods.

# C. Baseline Details

For baseline approaches that involve stochasticity (i.e., all except BM25), we perform three runs with different random seeds and report the average performance and standard deviation.

# C.1.BM25

We implement the BM25 baseline with the rank_bm25<sup>11</sup> package. We employ the same approach to aggregating scores across subtasks as Equation (2), except using the BM25 scores instead of the gradient similarity scores.

# C.2. DSIR

DSIR (Xie et al., 2023b) offers a lightweight approach to select data based on specific target distributions using n-gram features. However, we find it unsuitable for our specific setting. The primary issue stems from the difficulty in learning a reliable n-gram model with the limited data available in our target distribution, leading to a sparse n-gram table. We hypothesize that this sparsity arises from the trade-off between bucket size and estimation accuracy for the source and target distribution: larger buckets result in a highly sparse table with unreliable estimations for data selection, while smaller buckets lead to more hash collisions for the data in the source distribution. In our empirical observations, we noticed that DSIR tends to prefer shorter sequences, which can harm performance (see Table 13 for similar evidence). The tendency of DSIR to select short sequences is also mentioned in Appendix J of Xie et al. (2023b), where the authors impose heuristic quality filters on top of the DSIR-selected datasets.

# D.2. Selecting Data for SGD, SignGD, and Adam Gradients

In order to compute the influence when training with the Adam optimizer (Definition 3.1), we need to access the Adam optimizer update  $\Gamma$ .  $\Gamma$  relies on the moving moment estimates that Adam tracks. In the early stages of training, Malladi et al. (2022) proved that the parameter updates induced by Adam can be approximated well by the sign of the gradient (this optimizer is known as SignGD). Intuitively, this is because the moment estimates  $m$  and  $v$  are close to their initialization and thus essentially perform coordinate-wise normalization on the gradient. Later empirical and theoretical in Malladi et al. (2023) suggested that fine-tuning language models occurs in the early stages of Adam, so signGD may be a decent approximation of fine-tuning LLMs with the Adam optimizer.

In the current paper, we obtain  $\Gamma$  through the optimizer states computed over the course of a short warmup training period. Here, we ablate our stated approach against approximating  $\Gamma$  as  $\mathrm{sign}(\nabla \ell (\cdot ;\pmb {\theta}))$ . One may also think this SignGD formulation could circumvent the gradient norm problems reported in §3.2.

One of our key contributions is to extend the influence formulation in §2 to operate with the Adam optimizer. However, due

to the approximations described above, the influences we compute in Definition 3.1 may not be as accurate as we hope. In that case, we may be better off using the more precise influences provided by the SGD formulation in Equation (1).

We empirically compare the efficacy of these three influence formulations (SGD, SignGD, and Adam) for data selection and present the results in Table 9. In this setup, we perform warmup training using all the available training data  $(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{warmup}} = \mathcal{D})$  and select the top  $5\%$  of the data for instruction tuning. In summary, we find that using Adam influence formulation generally matches or outperforms the SGD and SignGD formulations, demonstrating the utility of our optimizer-aware influence formulation in Definition 3.1.

Instruction tuning could degrade performance. In Table 10, we compare results from instruction tuning to the results directly from the base model without instruction tuning. Although instruction tuning generally enhances performance, there are instances where it may result in a decline. A notable example is observed in the MMLU dataset, where instruction tuning using the  $5\%$  data subset chosen by LESS yields slightly inferior results compared to the base model. This suggests that the chosen data may not significantly improve the performance of the target task for some models, especially more capable ones such as LLAMA-2-13B and MISTRAL-7B. Additionally, it could imply that the pool of training data we're selecting from lacks further beneficial examples for the specified task. Experimenting with a larger pool of instruction tuning could possibly benefit the target task more. Despite this, it is important to note that LESS remains competitive to other approaches. By scaling up the compute to have a better estimate of the influence, we expect that the performance of LESS will further improve, as indicated in Section 6.1.

Training with validation data rarely helps. An additional straightforward baseline involves using the few-shot validation data for training. Specifically, we train the model for 100 steps with the standard hyperparameters, and evaluate the model on the validation set for every 10 steps, and report the test score of the best checkpoint. The results of this approach are shown in Table 10. Here, we compare it with the performance of the standard LAMA-2-7B model and the outcomes when fine-tuning the model with either  $5\%$  of data chosen at random or  $5\%$  selected by LESS. We observe that training solely with in-domain validation data rarely provides a benefit over not training the model at all, and it even surpasses the performance achieved by randomly selecting  $5\% (13,000)$  data points. Nevertheless, LESS demonstrates a superior ability to identify data points more relevant to the target task, significantly outperforming the strategy of training with only validation data.

# D.4. Detailed Results on Using Different Projection Dimensions

Recall that the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma (Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984) states that random projections preserve inner products (e.g., the influence formulation in Definition 3.1) as long as the projected dimension  $d$  is large enough. Here, we

ablate against different projection dimensions to find what empirical choice of  $d$  is "large enough". In this setup, we perform warmup training using all the available training data  $(D_{\text{warmup}} = D)$  and select the top 5% of the data for instruction tuning. We present the detailed results of projecting the LoRA gradients to different dimensions including 1024, 2048, 4096 and 8192 in Table 11. We find that even using a small dimension still outperforms the random selection performance. Using a larger dimension for projected gradients is better. # E. Nuances in Algorithm Design

In Sections 2 and 3, we outline how first-order approximations of influence functions can be used to design optimizer-aware data selection algorithms. Here, we discuss some of the nuances in designing such an algorithm for the practical hyperparameter settings used in instruction tuning.

Batch Size. The per-step influence and trajectory-wise influence of a training datapoint  $z$  on a validation datapoint  $z'$  is characterized under the assumption that the batch size is 1. Standard training setups use a larger batch size, but it is difficult to characterize how training on a batch of examples can change  $\ell(z'; \theta)$ . However, under the assumption that the first-order approximation is sufficiently powerful to describe training dynamics, we can directly use the linearity of the gradient to derive the influence of a single datapoint within a batch. In particular, when training with SGD on a batch  $B_t$  of size  $B$ , we can write:

$$
\begin{array}{l} \ell (\pmb {z} ^ {\prime}; \pmb {\theta} ^ {t + 1}) - \ell (\pmb {z} ^ {\prime}; \pmb {\theta} ^ {t}) \approx - \eta_ {t} \langle \nabla \ell (\pmb {z} ^ {\prime}; \pmb {\theta} ^ {t}), \nabla \ell (B _ {t}; \pmb {\theta} ^ {t}) \rangle \\ = - \frac {\eta_ {t}}{B} \sum_ {\boldsymbol {x} \in B _ {t}} \left\langle \nabla \ell \left(\boldsymbol {z} ^ {\prime}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}\right), \nabla \ell \left(\boldsymbol {x}; \boldsymbol {\theta} ^ {t}\right) \right\rangle \\ \end{array}
$$

An analogous formula follows straightforwardly for Adam. The  $1 / B$  factor is shared across all datapoints and thus does not affect the ranking of the matching scores. Therefore, selecting examples as if the batch size were 1 is the same as selecting examples for a larger batch size. Intuitively, this is because the interaction between examples within a batch must be negligible in order for the first-order approximation to be accurate. We remark that the equal division of the influence across constituents of a mini-batch cannot circumvent the gradient norm problem presented in §3.2. We discuss this point in more detail in Appendix H.

Learning Rate Schedule. Instruction tuning generally uses a linearly decaying learning rate schedule, which means that each example (or batch) within an epoch has a different learning rate depending on the time step that it was sampled. The formulation in Definition 3.1 may then select a datapoint solely because it would be sampled earlier in the training run (i.e., with a larger  $\eta_t$ ). We want our method to yield an interpretable dataset, so we instead use the average epoch-wise learning rate as  $\eta_t$ .

# F. Comparison to "Kernel Behavior" (Malladi et al., 2023; Jacot et al., 2018).

The design of LESS relies on the assumption that the first-order approximation of loss dynamics is sufficiently accurate (see Section 2). A related hypothesis is the idea that the instruction tuning optimization procedure exhibits "kernel

behavior".<sup>12</sup> Here, we highlight the nuanced difference between kernel behavior and the current assumption, emphasizing why kernel behavior is difficult to measure in the instruction tuning setting.

**Formal Difference** Kernel behavior describes the gradient of the output of the model (i.e., the logits) with respect to the model parameters. In contrast, the first-order approximation in Section 2 is for the gradient of the loss with respect to the parameters. These two quantities are related by the derivative of the loss with respect to the output of the model. Thus, conceptually, kernel behavior and the first-order approximation of the loss dynamics are similar, but the two phenomena correspond to different formal descriptions of model training. Theory in Yang & Hu (2021); Malladi et al. (2023) indicates that there is some subtlety in whether one phenomenon implies the other holds. In particular, most kernel-based analyses minimize the mean squared error loss instead of the cross-entropy loss, the latter of which introduces technical complications in the analysis but is the standard choice for instruction tuning.

# F.1. Does instruction tuning exhibit kernel behavior?

Malladi et al. (2023) suggested that fine-tuning on GLUE-style classification tasks can sometimes exhibit kernel behavior because the pre-trained model is already fairly strong at the downstream task. This assumption crucially relied on appending a very good prompt to the downstream task input, so that labeling the input amounted to predicting the next word. With such a prompt, fine-tuning on the downstream task amounted to the same as continued pre-training. One could argue that the instruction in instruction tuning provides a prompt for the completion, but the completion can be much longer than the single-word labels used in classification tasks.

Gradient features change substantially at the start of instruction tuning. We perform ablations comparing gradient features collected from the pre-trained model and features collected over the course of the warmup training period (Table 6). We find that it is important to incorporate gradients taken over the course of the warmup training, which suggests that the gradient features change substantially at the start of instruction tuning. On the other hand, results in Table 5 demonstrate that lengthening the warmup period yields marginal gains in performance, suggesting that the gradient features don't change much in the intermediate and late phases of instruction tuning. We emphasize that especially in the case of the cross entropy objective, it is possible for the gradient features in Definition 3.1 to change substantially even if the model exhibits kernel behavior. It is thus difficult to relate the loss curves in Figure 5 to the evolution of the empirical neural tangent kernel over the course of instruction tuning.

Direct verification is too challenging. Kernel behavior is called as such because the dynamics are described by a fixed  $|\mathcal{D}| \times |\mathcal{D}|$  kernel, where each entry contains the inner product of the gradients of the model output with respect to the parameters for different datapoints. Subtly, this matrix must be constructed for every logit, so the computational cost scales with the number of classes in the dataset. Past works that instantiate this kernel have focused on vision tasks with relatively few classes (Arora et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020a; Wei et al., 2022a) or prompt-based text classification (Malladi et al., 2023), which only considers a label word for each class in simple downstream tasks. On the other hand, instruction tuning requires a  $|\mathcal{V}|$ -dimensional vector of logits from the model, where  $\mathcal{V}$  is the vocabulary containing, for example, roughly 32,000 tokens. Additionally, each partial context must be treated as its own input, making  $|\mathcal{D}|$  the length of each sequence multiplied by the number of sequences in the dataset. Therefore, computing the kernel for instruction tuning is computationally infeasible, given the massive size of  $\mathcal{D}$  and the large number of output classes.

# G. Comparison to Data Models (Ilyas et al., 2022)

Concurrent work in Engstrom et al. (2024) applies the datamodels framework (Ilyas et al., 2022) to select pre-training data to induce strong performance on target downstream tasks. Fitting a datamodel generally requires repeated training of a model to fit a mapping between the selected data and the resulting accuracy of the trained model, but Park et al. (2023) reframed the problem to simulate this repeated training procedure at a much lower computational cost. Datamodels and its related techniques are inspired by influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017), which permit counterfactual reasoning over training (e.g., "what would have happened if I omitted this training datapoint?"). On the other hand, the influence formulation in Pruthi et al. (2020) can only counterfactually reason over one optimization step at a time, not the entire training procedure.

In this section, we describe the how the data selection method in Engstrom et al. (2024), which was applied to the pre-training

setting, relates to the considerations and observations reported in this paper.

1. Reframing data selection as optimization: Both works reframe the data selection problem as a straightforward optimization problem, whereby one chooses data to maximally improve a given metric. This approach is in contrast to prior works that rely on pre-defined notions of useful data (Gururangan et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023b) or surface-form features (Xie et al., 2023b). Instead, both works can be considered efficient approximations of meta-optimization approaches (Nguyen et al., 2021).  
2. Approximating training dynamics: Given the considerable computational cost of training LLMs, both works have to approximate the training dynamics of the model in order to efficiently estimate the effect of including a datapoint LESS relies on the assumption that the first-order approximation of the loss dynamics is accurate, whereas Engstrom et al. (2024) relies on a first-order approximation of how the model output changes during training. The latter is closer to the kernel view described in the previous section, though we note that the output used in datamodels generally involves the margin, which is not well-covered by kernel theory. An implicit assumption of both of these techniques is that the influences of selected datapoints add linearly (i.e., selecting two equally highly scored datapoints will doubly improve the model performance). Theory in Saunshi et al. (2023) provides a start at understanding when this linearity holds, but it may not apply to all settings. For example, in the case where the same datapoint is duplicated in the training set, we don't expect a marked improvement from the inclusion of the duplicate.  
3. Proxy models and selection models: Both works require performing initial training in a smaller, computationally efficient setup before performing data selection. Engstrom et al. (2024) trained smaller so-called proxy models to fit the datamodel, whereas LESS requires a short warmup training period using the selection model in order to compute the influence in Definition 3.1.  
4. Sequence selection versus token selection: In §3.2, we describe how selecting sequences instead of individual tokens requires an adjustment to the influence formulation. The token versus sequence consideration also appears in Engstrom et al. (2024), where the method has to average the model output over the entire sequence before using it in the data model. Because Engstrom et al. (2024) considers the pre-training setting, where all input sequences are of the same (maximal) length, we cannot be sure if their proposed methodology circumvents the sequence length bias described in §3.2.

# H. Sequence Level Gradients in Instruction Tuning

In Section 3.2, we discussed that sequence level gradients in instruction tuning exhibit odd properties because they are the average of the token gradients in the completion. In particular, Figure 3 demonstrates that across datasets, the gradient norm is strongly anti-correlated with the length of the completion. Similarly, Figure 4 shows that the norm of the update  $\| \Gamma \|$  (see Definition 3.1) decays rapidly with length. Therefore, using the straightforward inner product formulation in Section 2 would bias LESS to select short sequences for the training dataset. Intuitively, training on only short sequences would prevent the model from learning to perform long-form generations, as is required for many tasks. Ablation experiments in Table 13 demonstrate that using inner products for LESS results in a dataset with much shorter sequences. Training on this dataset induces markedly worse performance than using the cosine similarity and sometimes worse performance than random selection. Wang et al. (2020) similarly reported that using dot products for data selection in the machine translation setting was less stable than using cosine similarity.

Importantly, this phenomenon is not a failure of the influence formulation in Section 2. Rather, it suggests that we ought to perform token-wise data selection (e.g., only averaging the loss over a few tokens within each sequence), because some gradients within a sequence are helpful and others are not. At the time of writing, such fine-grained selection is computationally intractable for LLMs, but additional approximations and implementation accelerations may make it possible.

# I. Loss vs Accuracy

Our motivation in Section 2 is to select datapoints that will most dramatically reduce the validation loss. However, for many language modeling tasks, it is unclear how minimizing the cross entropy interacts with the task accuracy. In particular, many complex tasks require the model to generate long-form sequences of text, and it is not clear how well long-form generation capability correlates with accuracy.

In Figures 6 to 8, we provide evidence across model scales and tasks that the minimizing the validation loss does not always correspond to increasing the accuracy. A similar phenomenon was observed in Zhou et al. (2023); Tay et al. (2021). Despite its reliance on the cross entropy loss, LESS boosts accuracy over relevant baselines. Overall, we suggest that optimizing alternative objectives may yield better accuracy, but we leave exploring such ideas to future work. We note that any objective function can be substituted into the derivation in Section 2, so the ideas used in developing LESS are easily extensible.
